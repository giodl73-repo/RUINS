# Access Substitution Checklist

Use this checklist when original access is replaced, supplemented, or mediated
by digital surrogates, replicas, casts, facsimiles, 3D prints, VR/AR scenes,
photogrammetry models, scans, digital twins, haptic devices, tactile copies,
visitor routes, viewing windows, remote cameras, apps, virtual tours,
conservation closures, teaching copies, or restitution/repatriation substitutes.

## Required Separations

| Layer | Required custody question |
|---|---|
| Original access | What direct access to original fabric, context, scale, light, sound, touch, route, or community use is absent or restricted? |
| Substitute form | Is the substitute digital image, model, replica, cast, facsimile, print, tactile copy, remote view, app, route, or exhibit? |
| Capture basis | What scan, photograph, drawing, measurement, archival record, cast, manual model, or interpretation created it? |
| Transformation | What scaling, smoothing, coloring, completion, cropping, compression, material change, route change, or interface choice altered it? |
| Metadata/paradata | Are sources, decisions, uncertainty, rights, custodians, file versions, and production steps recorded? |
| Use purpose | Is the substitute for conservation, education, accessibility, research, restitution, tourism, emergency access, or display? |
| Missing affordance | What cannot be accessed: material, texture, weight, context, smell, sound, movement, risk, ritual, ownership, or governance? |
| Governance | Who authorized, owns, curates, restricts, profits from, or contests the substitute? |
| Evaluation | What expert review, community review, user study, conservation audit, or comparison test supports the access claim? |
| Public memory | Are "same as being there," "saved forever," "returned digitally," and "complete access" stories contained? |

## Mandatory Refusals

A RUINS paper must refuse:

1. surrogate as original;
2. replica as object-equivalent fabric;
3. digital access as full site access;
4. virtual tour as original movement or experience;
5. 3D print as complete material, scale, texture, weight, or context;
6. remote view as conservation status or direct inspection;
7. digital restitution as physical return, governance settlement, or community
   consent;
8. substitute availability as complete preservation, research closure, or public
   access equity.

## Cross-Case Test

| Test | Digital surrogate preservation | Replica significance | 3D/digital authenticity |
|---|---|---|---|
| Source condition | Digital objects need contextual metadata and preservation custody. | Replicas can acquire value and histories of their own. | 3D reproduction changes authenticity, aura, materiality, and access relations. |
| Evidence pressure | Surrogate becomes original access. | Replica value becomes object equivalence. | Digital authenticity becomes complete heritage recovery. |
| Core rule | Substitute access must be labelled by purpose, capture, transformation, governance, missing affordance, and evaluation. | Substitute history is its own evidence layer. | Access substitution does not close original custody. |

## Automatic Cap

A paper cannot score above `34` if it treats a digital surrogate, scan, model,
replica, cast, facsimile, 3D print, VR/AR scene, haptic device, tactile copy,
remote camera, digital twin, virtual tour, app, viewing window, visitor route,
teaching copy, or restitution substitute as the original object, original
context, object-equivalent fabric, full site access, original movement,
original experience, direct inspection, complete preservation, physical return,
governance settlement, community consent, research closure, or public access
equity without this checklist or an equivalent access-substitution gate.

## Source Pressure

- Digital preservation work foregrounds contextual metadata, rights, provenance,
  and relationships between original objects and digital cultural objects.
- Replica studies show that replicas can gain heritage value while remaining
  distinct objects with their own histories.
- 3D/digital authenticity work foregrounds the limits of reproduction,
  materiality, institutional purpose, and curatorial transparency.
