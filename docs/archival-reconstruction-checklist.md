# Archival Reconstruction Checklist

Use this checklist when destroyed architecture or urban fabric is rebuilt from
rubble, surviving fabric, archival plans, photographs, paintings, inventories,
photogrammetry, structural modeling, expert interpretation, or public memory.

## Required Separations

| Surface | Required custody question |
|---|---|
| Destruction record | What event, process, or later clearance produced the loss? |
| Surviving fabric | Which stones, walls, cellars, street lines, foundations, or interiors survived directly? |
| Salvaged material | Which fragments were recovered, mapped, catalogued, reused, or left out? |
| Archival source | Is the claim based on plans, floor plans, photographs, paintings, inventories, or reconstruction office records? |
| Model or survey | Did photogrammetry, computer modeling, structural analysis, or scanning mediate the reconstruction claim? |
| New fabric | Which parts are new stone, modern structure, replacement craft, or corrected engineering? |
| Adaptation | Which interiors, services, structures, or urban functions were changed for modern use? |
| Public memory | Is peace, recovery, reconciliation, national memory, or World Heritage status being handled as afterlife rather than proof of original state? |

## Refuse Unless Separately Locked

- Rebuilding proves complete original fabric.
- Reused stones make the reconstructed whole object-equivalent.
- Historical plans or photographs prove exact interior recovery.
- A computer model is direct access to the lost building.
- Structural correction is the same as original construction logic.
- Peace, reconciliation, or recovery memory proves total heritage recovery.
- World Heritage or official recognition settles final conservation doctrine.
