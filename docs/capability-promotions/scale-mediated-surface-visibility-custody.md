## Overclaim Pressure

Large surface traces are often known through scales that ancient viewers may not
have occupied: aerial photography, satellite imagery, radar, LiDAR, public
outlines, and AI candidate maps. Nasca tempts readers to treat aerial legibility,
remote-sensing discovery, and map outlines as proof of ancient intended
viewpoint, complete inventory, final ritual function, or complete damage
assessment. Uffington adds a different surface condition: a chalk hill figure
whose visible shape depends on slope, distance, scouring, re-cutting,
restoration, drone/aerial survey, and OSL dating.

## Scope Decision

Current paper permission:
- Allowed: Nasca as a vast surface-trace case where public knowledge depends on
  scale, viewpoint, survey, imagery, and conservation mediation.
- Refused: aerial visibility as ancient intended viewpoint, AI candidate as
  confirmed geoglyph, complete inventory, final ritual/astronomical function,
  total damage map, and whole Nasca culture explanation.
- Core rule: no paper may treat aerial/drone/satellite image, survey outline,
  re-cut/restoration profile, remote-sensing candidate, or public map as ancient
  viewpoint, complete original shape, final date, final function, complete
  damage state, or whole-landscape interpretation without the checklist.
- Sibling handoff: PORTO owns geography; CERES owns geomorphology, rainfall,
  radar, LiDAR, and conservation science; CANON owns named geoglyph identity;
  RITE owns ritual interpretation; MAXIM/LUCIA own broader Paracas/Nasca
  history; AI/remote-sensing systems own model implementation.
