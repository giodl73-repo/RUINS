# Visitor Route Evidence Custody

**Capability id:** `ruins-capability:visitor-route-evidence-custody`
**Promotion:** Candidate -> Active -> Core
**Trigger pressure:** visitor-flow management, archaeological tourism
guidance, and interpretive planning at heritage sites
**Rubric:** v1.70

## Problem

Visitor routes are necessary for conservation, safety, accessibility, and public
interpretation. They also strongly shape what a ruin appears to be. Visitor
paths, boardwalks, platforms, barriers, closures, queues, timed-entry routes,
guide scripts, signs, lighting plans, visitor centres, digital tours, crowd-flow
models, carrying-capacity estimates, accessibility substitutes, social media
routes, and interpretive sequences can collapse into original circulation,
ancient intended viewpoints, original boundaries, ritual sequence, population
movement, direct original access, archaeological significance, complete site
experience, original experience, or final conservation status.

## Core Rule

Visitor-route claims must keep original access, excavated exposure, modern
route, management purpose, view frame, wear and impact, interpretation layer,
accessibility, visitor data, conservation status, and public memory separate.

## Required Controls

- Visitor-route evidence gate.
- Source lock.
- Page-anchor audit.
- Refusal register.
- Cultural-memory register.
- Sibling-boundary register.
- Cross-case checklist.

## Source Pressure

| Source family | Pressure exposed |
|---|---|
| Visitor-flow management | Modern flows are planned to reduce negative impacts and improve benefits, not to reveal original circulation. |
| Archaeological tourism guidance | Tour routes, site management, guides, visitor behavior, and protection are separate from archaeological evidence. |
| Interpretive planning | Desired visitor experiences and accessibility choices are current planning layers, not original site experience. |

## Decision

Promote directly to Core under rubric v1.70. Visitor route evidence custody
fills the Site Damage, Access, and Conservation Afterlife gap by treating modern
public circulation as a managed access layer rather than proof of original
movement, intended view, authentic experience, or final conservation state.
