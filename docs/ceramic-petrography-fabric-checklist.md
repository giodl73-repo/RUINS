# Ceramic Petrography Fabric Checklist

Use this checklist when ceramic provenance, production, exchange, technology,
workshop, recipe, firing, clay source, temper, fabric group, or interaction
claims rely on thin-section petrography, polarizing microscopy, ceramic fabric
classification, mineral inclusions, voids, clay matrix, aplastic inclusions,
grain size, optical mineralogy, SEM, XRD, NAA, pXRF/XRF, geochemistry, image
classification, or automated fabric recognition.

## Required Separations

| Layer | Required custody question |
|---|---|
| Sherd/object | Which vessel, sherd, tile, brick, figurine, clay object, or ceramic assemblage was sampled? |
| Context | What layer, site, feature, collection, survey, or museum context anchors the sample? |
| Sampling design | Which objects were sampled, excluded, destructively sectioned, or represented only macroscopically? |
| Thin section | How was the sample cut, mounted, thicknessed, imaged, and described under PPL/XP or equivalent modes? |
| Fabric description | What clay matrix, inclusions, voids, temper, texture, orientation, and firing features define the fabric? |
| Grouping | Is the fabric group petrographic, chemical, typological, stylistic, functional, chronological, or mixed? |
| Raw material comparison | Are local clays, geological sources, tempers, wasters, kilns, and reference collections available? |
| Analytical pairing | Are petrography, geochemistry, SEM/XRD, residue, form, decoration, and context kept in separate claim grades? |
| Production inference | Is the claim clay choice, recipe, firing, forming, workshop habit, or broader craft organization? |
| Provenance inference | Is the claim local/nonlocal, source zone, workshop, route, exchange system, migration, or identity? |
| Public memory | Are "trade route," "foreign potter," "ethnic ware," and "ancient industry" stories contained? |

## Mandatory Refusals

A RUINS paper must refuse:

1. fabric group as exact workshop;
2. petrographic match as direct trade route;
3. mineral inclusion as single clay source without source comparison;
4. chemical group as geological provenance without fabric/context custody;
5. thin section as complete recipe or firing method;
6. automated fabric class as expert interpretation;
7. style/fabric overlap as ethnicity, migration, or political boundary;
8. small sampled subset as complete assemblage, market, production system, or
   interaction sphere.

## Cross-Case Test

| Test | Ceramic petrography method | NAA/petrography combined studies | Automated fabric classification | Core rule |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Source condition | Thin sections describe matrix, inclusions, texture, and technology. | Bulk chemistry and petrography classify related but different material signals. | Image models classify thin-section fabrics from training data. | Sample, fabric, chemistry, model, and archaeological inference layers must stay separate. |
| Evidence pressure | Fabric similarity becomes provenance or workshop. | Chemical group becomes source despite geological ambiguity. | Model class becomes expert-grade fabric interpretation. | Group membership is not route, workshop, identity, or full production history. |
| Overclaim pressure | Petrography becomes complete production recipe. | Multi-analysis becomes exact trade map. | Automated classification becomes solved provenance at scale. | Provenance remains probabilistic and source-comparison dependent. |

## Automatic Cap

A paper cannot score above `34` if it treats ceramic thin section, fabric group,
clay matrix, mineral inclusion, temper, void, firing texture, SEM/XRD result,
NAA group, pXRF/XRF cluster, geochemical match, image classification, or
automated fabric model as exact workshop, direct trade route, certain clay
source, complete recipe, firing method, production system, ethnicity, migration,
political boundary, market map, or complete interaction sphere without this
checklist or an equivalent ceramic petrography fabric gate.

## Source Pressure

- Ceramic petrography method papers foreground thin-section description,
  sample selection, matrix/inclusion interpretation, fabric grouping, and
  provenance/technology inference as separate layers.
- Combined NAA and petrography studies show that chemical groupings and
  geological provenance can diverge even when raw clays are sampled.
- Automated ceramic fabric classification papers foreground training data,
  image classes, expert labels, and model scope as separate custody layers.
