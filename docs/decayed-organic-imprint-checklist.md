# Decayed Organic Imprint Checklist

Use this checklist when vanished organic material is known through stains,
postholes, voids, fasteners, soil chemistry, durable associated objects, markers,
replicas, or public reconstruction images.

## Required Separations

| Surface | Required custody question |
|---|---|
| Original organic fabric | Is any original wood, textile, body, thatch, rope, or leather preserved? |
| Imprint, stain, or posthole | What survives directly: shape, position, fill, chemical trace, fastener pattern, or void? |
| Durable associated objects | Are metalwork, stone, bone, pottery, or grave goods direct context, displaced finds, or display objects? |
| Marker or replica | Does the public see original evidence, a concrete marker, a sculpture, a reconstruction, or a visualization? |
| Excavation and recording | Are exact dimensions, alignments, and plans source-locked to excavation reports? |
| Identity and body inference | Is a body, person, group, or maker inferred from absence, chemistry, objects, or later tradition? |
| Function and ritual | Is the proposed use source-locked, comparative, speculative, or public-memory language? |
| Landscape and culture | Is the case being used as a complete culture story rather than a bounded evidence condition? |

## Refuse Unless Separately Locked

- The imprint or marker is the complete original organic object.
- Fasteners or postholes alone provide a complete plan.
- Soil chemistry supplies identity, biography, or cause of death.
- Associated objects supply a full ritual script.
- Replica or marker restores direct access to original fabric.
- Public memory or comparison proves final function.
- One organic-imprint case explains a whole culture.
