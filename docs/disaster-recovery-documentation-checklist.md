# Disaster Recovery Documentation Checklist

Use this checklist when a ruin, archive, museum, monument, collection, historic
district, sacred site, archaeological site, or cultural landscape is interpreted
through emergency plans, disaster risk management files, rapid damage
assessments, first-aid forms, triage records, emergency photographs, satellite
or drone images, incident logs, salvage priorities, stabilization records,
recovery plans, reconstruction proposals, funding documents, or community
recovery narratives.

## Required Separations

| Layer | Required custody question |
|---|---|
| Pre-disaster baseline | What inventory, survey, condition record, map, photograph, or management file documents the pre-event state? |
| Hazard event | What flood, fire, earthquake, storm, conflict, landslide, looting, neglect, or cascading event is being described? |
| Access condition | Who could inspect the site, when, under what safety, conflict, weather, legal, or logistical limits? |
| Rapid assessment | What form, checklist, image, route, sample, or remote observation produced the first damage claim? |
| Damage category | Is the claim partial damage, total loss, displaced element, contamination, instability, theft, exposure, or unknown status? |
| Triage decision | What was prioritized for rescue, stabilization, documentation, storage, evacuation, or later treatment? |
| Salvage record | Which object, fragment, file, sample, or fabric was moved, labelled, stored, treated, or left in place? |
| Recovery plan | Is the plan emergency response, temporary stabilization, conservation, reconstruction, memorialization, or urban recovery? |
| Funding/governance | Which agency, donor, insurer, community, owner, custodian, or emergency authority controls the action? |
| Update cycle | What later inspection, monitoring, inventory reconciliation, or audit changes the first report? |
| Public memory | Are total-loss, heroic rescue, neglect, rebuild-now, and recovered-whole stories contained? |

## Mandatory Refusals

A RUINS paper must refuse:

1. rapid damage assessment as complete loss inventory;
2. emergency photograph as total condition map;
3. no-access zone as absence proof;
4. salvage priority as cultural value ranking;
5. stabilization action as completed conservation;
6. recovery plan as reconstruction authority;
7. funding approval as historical or community consensus;
8. public before/after image as complete destruction or complete recovery.

## Cross-Case Test

| Test | Post-disaster damage assessment | First aid to cultural heritage | Disaster risk management |
|---|---|---|---|
| Source condition | Fast assessments are made under safety, access, and capacity constraints. | First-aid workflows triage, secure, document, and stabilize affected heritage. | Risk manuals separate preparedness, response, damage assessment, treatment, recovery, and rehabilitation. |
| Evidence pressure | Initial forms become final inventories. | Emergency stabilization becomes full conservation. | Recovery plan becomes completed recovery or reconstruction right. |
| Core rule | Disaster records are time-bound, access-limited, and update-dependent evidence. | Triage is not value, completeness, or final status. | Preparedness, response, treatment, recovery, and memory must remain separate. |

## Automatic Cap

A paper cannot score above `34` if it treats an emergency plan, rapid damage
assessment, first-aid form, incident log, drone image, satellite image,
emergency photograph, no-access report, triage list, salvage priority,
stabilization record, recovery plan, reconstruction proposal, funding document,
or before/after public image as complete loss inventory, total condition map,
absence proof, cultural value ranking, completed conservation, reconstruction
authority, community consensus, complete destruction, complete recovery, or
solved disaster history without this checklist or an equivalent disaster
recovery documentation gate.

## Source Pressure

- ICCROM post-disaster damage-assessment work foregrounds capacity, timing,
  access, risk, and recovery gaps in first assessments.
- First Aid to Cultural Heritage in Times of Crisis separates coordination,
  assessment, security, salvage, stabilization, and recovery actions.
- World Heritage disaster-risk management separates preparedness, response,
  damage assessment, treatment, recovery, and rehabilitation as linked but
  distinct stages.
