# Glass/Faience Compositional Checklist

Use this checklist when glass, faience, frit, glaze, bead, vessel, ingot,
mosaic tessera, amulet, pigment, colorant, opacifier, flux, plant ash, natron,
sand, quartz, alkali, cobalt, copper, antimony, lead, tin, Sr/Nd/Pb isotope,
trace element, LA-ICP-MS, SEM-EDS, EPMA, Raman, XRF, pXRF, or compositional
group is used to reconstruct source, recipe, workshop, technology, exchange,
trade, recycling, color, chronology, identity, or cultural interaction.

## Required Separations

| Layer | Required custody question |
|---|---|
| Object/sample | Which glass, faience, frit, glaze, bead, vessel, ingot, tessera, amulet, fragment, or production waste is sampled? |
| Context | What excavation, collection, hoard, shipwreck, workshop, tomb, layer, or museum record anchors the object? |
| Material class | Is the material natron glass, plant-ash glass, faience, frit, glaze, pigment, recycled cullet, or mixed fabric? |
| Analytical method | Which SEM-EDS, EPMA, LA-ICP-MS, Raman, XRF/pXRF, isotope, microscopy, or bulk method produced the data? |
| Component signal | Which element or isotope reflects sand, plant ash, natron, colorant, opacifier, fuel, contamination, or corrosion? |
| Weathering | Could leaching, hydration, devitrification, burial alteration, conservation, or surface corrosion affect the result? |
| Grouping | Is the group compositional, technological, chronological, typological, color-based, isotopic, or provenance-based? |
| Reference set | What sands, ashes, natron sources, ores, workshops, ingots, cullet, or databases support comparison? |
| Recycling/mixing | Are cullet reuse, colorant contamination, opacifier carryover, batch mixing, and remelting separately assessed? |
| Production inference | Is the claim raw-glass making, secondary working, glazing, faience body preparation, colorant recipe, or workshop practice? |
| Provenance inference | Is the claim source zone, production region, workshop, trade route, exchange network, migration, or identity? |
| Public memory | Are "ancient trade," "lost recipe," "royal color," "foreign luxury," and solved-industry stories contained? |

## Mandatory Refusals

A RUINS paper must refuse:

1. compositional group as exact workshop;
2. isotope match as direct trade route;
3. colorant or opacifier as complete recipe;
4. pXRF surface result as bulk composition without weathering controls;
5. faience glaze/body chemistry as complete production method;
6. trace element contamination as primary source without recycling audit;
7. raw-glass source as vessel-maker or owner;
8. glass/faience composition as ethnicity, status, migration, political
   control, or complete industry history.

## Cross-Case Test

| Test | Element/isotope review | Plant-ash glass provenance | Egyptian faience composition |
|---|---|---|---|
| Source condition | Elements and isotopes can reflect raw material, colorant, opacifier, recycling, and alteration signals. | Sr/Nd isotope and trace-element fields suggest production zones and decentralization. | Multi-analytical faience work separates body, glaze, colorant, and uncertain origin. |
| Evidence pressure | Chemical signal becomes source or trade route. | Production zone becomes route, workshop, or political economy. | Composition becomes complete recipe or cultural meaning. |
| Core rule | Component signal, alteration, grouping, reference set, and inference scale must stay separate. | Provenance remains probabilistic and source-set dependent. | Technology claims require body/glaze/process separation. |

## Automatic Cap

A paper cannot score above `34` if it treats glass, faience, frit, glaze,
colorant, opacifier, natron, plant ash, sand, isotope ratio, trace element,
LA-ICP-MS result, SEM-EDS result, EPMA result, Raman spectrum, XRF/pXRF cluster,
compositional group, source match, cullet signal, or recycling marker as exact
workshop, direct trade route, complete recipe, final production method, vessel
maker, owner, ethnicity, status, migration, political control, complete
industry, solved provenance, or lost technology without this checklist or an
equivalent glass/faience compositional gate.

## Source Pressure

- Ancient-glass element/isotope reviews foreground that different elements and
  isotopes can reflect raw materials, colorants, opacifiers, contamination,
  recycling, and alteration rather than one simple provenance signal.
- Islamic plant-ash glass studies show that isotopic and trace-element fields
  can define probable production zones while leaving route, workshop, and
  exchange claims probabilistic.
- Egyptian faience composition studies foreground body, glaze, colorant,
  technology, and uncertain-origin issues as separate evidence layers.
