# Haptic Reconstruction Checklist

Use this checklist when touch, handling, texture, weight, force feedback,
virtual handling, 3D-printed replicas, tactile maps, touch tables, haptic
augmented reality, or accessibility replicas are used to reconstruct access to
an archaeological object, surface, building, landscape, inscription, or museum
experience.

## Required Separations

| Layer | Required custody question |
|---|---|
| Original object | Which object, surface, inscription, cast, building, or landscape is the source? |
| Conservation status | Why is the original untouchable, restricted, fragile, inaccessible, or mediated? |
| Capture method | Was the form captured by laser scan, photogrammetry, CT, manual modelling, drawing, or interpretive reconstruction? |
| Geometry | What resolution, smoothing, missing area, scale change, or registration error affects the shape? |
| Surface texture | Is texture measured, modeled, simplified, exaggerated, printed, or inferred from visual data? |
| Material and weight | Does the replica preserve material, density, temperature, balance, flexibility, or only external form? |
| Haptic device | Is touch delivered by physical replica, force feedback, vibration, mid-air haptics, glove, stylus, or illusion? |
| Alignment | Are visual, physical, and haptic layers co-located, scaled, delayed, or offset? |
| User study | What user group, task, accessibility need, expert review, or novice response tested the experience? |
| Access claim | Is the claim tactile access, learning aid, conservation substitute, research aid, or original-object experience? |
| Public memory | Are "touching the past," "holding the original," and direct-presence stories contained? |

## Mandatory Refusals

A RUINS paper must refuse:

1. haptic feedback as direct touch of the original object;
2. 3D-printed replica as object-equivalent fabric;
3. tactile access as complete sensory recovery;
4. texture rendering as ancient surface feel without material and wear custody;
5. force feedback as original weight, resistance, balance, or use condition;
6. accessibility replica as solved conservation, provenance, or interpretation;
7. user enthusiasm as evidence of archaeological accuracy;
8. virtual handling as proof of ancient embodied experience, craft technique,
   ritual use, or social meaning.

## Cross-Case Test

| Test | Haptics in digital archaeology | Haptic augmented reality | 3D-printed tactile replicas | Core rule |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Source condition | Real artefact compared with visual-haptic representation. | Untouchable museum artefact explored through haptic illusion. | Museum objects digitized and reproduced for touch access. | Original, capture, replica, device, and access layers must be distinct. |
| Evidence pressure | Haptic feedback can reveal features but novices may overrate similarity. | Co-located visual/tactile effects can feel like direct manipulation. | Printed form can improve accessibility while changing material, weight, and surface. | User experience is not object equivalence. |
| Overclaim pressure | Digital touch becomes archaeological reading. | Illusion becomes touching the artefact. | Replica becomes complete sensory substitute. | Haptic access is mediated reconstruction, not direct contact or original feel. |

## Automatic Cap

A paper cannot score above `34` if it treats haptic feedback, tactile replica,
3D print, touch table, glove, stylus, vibration, force feedback, haptic
augmented reality, or virtual handling as direct original contact, complete
sensory recovery, object-equivalent fabric, exact surface feel, ancient use
experience, solved accessibility, or final interpretation without this
checklist or an equivalent haptic reconstruction gate.

## Source Pressure

- Haptics in digital archaeology studies show that haptic feedback can support
  exploration while raising similarity, accuracy, and expert/novice calibration
  issues.
- Haptic augmented reality work foregrounds the curatorial need to provide
  touch-like access without allowing the interface to become the original
  artefact.
- 3D-printed tactile-replica work foregrounds accessibility benefits while
  changing material, scale, texture, surface, and conservation status.
