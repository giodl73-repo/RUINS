# Lost Inventory Reconstruction Checklist

Use this checklist when a lost, dispersed, burned, looted, sold, donated,
confiscated, reassembled, digitized, or partially surviving collection is
reconstructed from catalogues, shelf lists, accession books, sale catalogues,
dealer records, shipping lists, donation lists, estate inventories, owner marks,
fragments, quotations, citations, library stamps, marginalia, bindings, digital
databases, provenance indexes, or later summaries.

## Required Separations

| Layer | Required custody question |
|---|---|
| Original collection | Which library, archive, museum, cabinet, hoard, cache, corpus, or private collection is being reconstructed? |
| Inventory witness | What catalogue, list, register, sale record, finding aid, shelfmark system, or database preserves the item claim? |
| Witness date | When was the witness made relative to acquisition, use, loss, dispersal, sale, destruction, or reconstruction? |
| Item identity | Is the claim exact item, title, work, edition, copy, volume, fragment, class, shelf position, or uncertain match? |
| Ownership signal | What ex libris, stamp, binding, annotation, shelfmark, catalogue number, or archival note supports ownership? |
| Transfer event | What gift, sale, theft, confiscation, inheritance, shipment, deaccession, or destruction changed custody? |
| Survival status | Is the item extant, missing, destroyed, dispersed, unidentified, duplicated, miscatalogued, or only reported? |
| Matching rule | How are variant titles, editions, languages, dates, aliases, fragments, and duplicate records reconciled? |
| Gap model | What part of the original inventory, catalogue, owner set, shelf order, or provenance chain is missing? |
| Reconstruction claim | Is the claim item existence, original holding, owner, route, collection profile, intellectual history, or full inventory? |
| Public memory | Are "lost library," "reunited collection," "complete catalogue," and cultural-loss stories contained? |

## Mandatory Refusals

A RUINS paper must refuse:

1. surviving catalogue as complete original inventory;
2. later sale record as original ownership;
3. title match as exact copy without item-level evidence;
4. provenance mark as uninterrupted custody;
5. fragment as whole work or whole collection;
6. database aggregation as digitally reunited collection;
7. missing record as proof of absence;
8. reconstructed inventory as complete intellectual world, loss total,
   restitution map, or collection biography.

## Cross-Case Test

| Test | Reconstructed personal library | Dispersed manuscript trade records | Medieval library database |
|---|---|---|---|
| Source condition | Newly found lists and annotations can identify holdings and dispositions. | Sale catalogues and dealer archives preserve uneven owner visibility. | Medieval catalogues and digital databases reconstruct former libraries from partial witnesses. |
| Evidence pressure | List becomes whole library. | Trade record becomes complete provenance or value history. | Database becomes complete medieval collection. |
| Core rule | Witness, item, copy, transfer, survival, gap, and reconstruction scale must stay separate. | Provenance reconstruction is asymmetrical and source-biased. | Digital reassembly is not original inventory closure. |

## Automatic Cap

A paper cannot score above `34` if it treats a catalogue, shelf list, accession
book, sale catalogue, dealer record, shipping list, donation list, estate
inventory, owner mark, fragment, quotation, citation, library stamp, marginalia,
binding, digital database, provenance index, or later summary as complete
original inventory, exact copy identity, uninterrupted custody, whole work,
whole collection, proof of absence, digitally reunited collection, restitution
map, total loss, complete intellectual world, or solved collection biography
without this checklist or an equivalent lost-inventory reconstruction gate.

## Source Pressure

- Reconstructed-library case studies foreground how newly found lists can
  identify holdings and disposition while leaving copy identity and inventory
  completeness gated.
- Manuscript-trade and provenance projects show that catalogues and sale records
  preserve uneven owner visibility and incomplete chains.
- Digital former-library projects foreground database aggregation as a research
  tool, not restored original order or complete collection closure.
