# Notated Performance Reconstruction Checklist

Use this checklist when a lost sound or embodied performance is reconstructed
from notation, song text, tablature, instrument evidence, transcription,
translation, edition, modern score, performance, or recording.

## Required Separations

| Surface | Required custody question |
|---|---|
| Material witness | Is the claim anchored to a tablet, papyrus, stone, manuscript, image, or recording carrier? |
| Text | Are lyrics, dedication, label, colophon, translation, and commentary separated from performance? |
| Notation system | Which signs, intervals, numbers, letters, dots, tuning terms, or movement signs survive? |
| Damage | Which signs, lines, edges, or context are missing, damaged, ground off, or unread? |
| Modern presentation | Is this a staff transcription, tablature, transliteration, translation, edition, or performance score? |
| Performance choice | Which melody, rhythm, tempo, tuning, instrument, voice, pronunciation, staging, or gesture is chosen by modern interpreters? |
| Recording or display | Is the public hearing an interpretation, reconstruction, arrangement, teaching aid, or artistic response? |
| Public memory | Is "oldest song," "playable," "complete," or "heard again" handled as afterlife rather than proof of direct recovery? |

## Refuse Unless Separately Locked

- Notation proves exact ancient sound.
- A modern score is object-equivalent to the ancient notation.
- A transcription settles final rhythm, tempo, tuning, or instrument timbre.
- A recording is the ancient performance.
- A complete inscription proves complete performance context.
- Public "oldest song" language proves direct playability.
- Translation or ritual reading supplies a complete performance setting.
