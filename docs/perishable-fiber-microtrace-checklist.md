# Perishable Fiber Microtrace Checklist

Use this checklist when cordage, weaving, clothing, basketry, hafting, dyeing,
skin/fur work, craft, textile production, or perishable technology is
reconstructed from microscopic fibers, flax, hair, twist, knots, cut ends,
color, dye signal, palynology, clay samples, cave sediment, microscopy, or
preservation context.

## Required Separations

| Surface | Required custody question |
|---|---|
| Sample | Which clay sample, sediment, feature, layer, unit, or microremain produced the fiber? |
| Layer | What dating, stratigraphy, disturbance, and intrusion controls are locked? |
| Fiber identity | Is the fiber flax, hair, plant, animal, mineral, modern contaminant, or unresolved? |
| Preservation | How did cave sediment, carbonate, humidity, burning, handling, and curation affect survival? |
| Contamination | Were modern fibers, excavation clothing, lab contamination, and sample handling controlled? |
| Modification | Are twist, spin, knot, cut end, breakage, fray, or natural morphology separately graded? |
| Color | Is the color dye, staining, mineralization, burning, degradation, or modern contamination? |
| Technique | Is the inference cordage, sewing, basketry, weaving, hafting, skin/fur work, or mixed? |
| Object | Is the claim fiber, thread, cord, textile, basket, garment, haft, or absent object? |
| Memory | Are oldest-textile, oldest-clothing, craft, fashion, and trade stories kept separate? |

## Refuse Unless Separately Locked

- A microscopic fiber is a complete textile.
- A twist is a whole cordage system.
- A colored fiber is dyeing technology.
- A cut end is a full sewing practice.
- Fiber presence is clothing.
- A few fibers prove craft specialization.
- Oldest-fiber memory is oldest-clothing history.
