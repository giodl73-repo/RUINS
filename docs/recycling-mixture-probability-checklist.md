# Recycling/Mixture Probability Checklist

Use this checklist when provenance, source, recipe, production, workshop,
trade, exchange, chronology, identity, or technology claims rely on materials
that may be recycled, remelted, alloyed, blended, reused, repaired, refired,
reworked, contaminated, mixed, batched, composited, or made from cullet, scrap,
secondary raw material, old metal, old glass, old ceramic, pigments, slags,
ores, tempers, residues, or conservation materials.

## Required Separations

| Layer | Required custody question |
|---|---|
| Object/sample | Which artefact, sherd, ingot, slag, bead, vessel, metal, glass, residue, pigment, or composite was sampled? |
| Primary signal | What component plausibly reflects original raw material, first production, geological source, or recipe? |
| Secondary signal | What component could reflect recycling, repair, remelting, alloying, mixing, reuse, contamination, or conservation? |
| Mixing mechanism | Is the mixture mechanical, chemical, thermal, batch-level, object-level, surface-level, corrosion-driven, or post-depositional? |
| Endmember model | Which source groups, raw materials, old materials, cullet, scrap, ores, ashes, clays, or additives form the candidate inputs? |
| Proportion estimate | Is there a quantified mixture fraction, qualitative likelihood, or only a possible mixture warning? |
| Analytical method | Which compositional, isotopic, microscopy, imaging, residue, or statistical method supports the mixture claim? |
| Statistical model | Are clustering, PCA, mixing lines, Bayesian models, outlier rules, and uncertainty reported separately? |
| Temporal layer | Does the material signal belong to first production, later repair, reuse, recycling, deposition, or conservation? |
| Provenance claim | Is the claim source, workshop, route, exchange, circulation, curation, repair, or mixed-material biography? |
| Public memory | Are "trade route," "lost recipe," "recycling economy," "collapse shortage," and solved-industry stories contained? |

## Mandatory Refusals

A RUINS paper must refuse:

1. mixed composition as single source;
2. recycled material as primary production evidence;
3. remelted glass as direct workshop provenance;
4. alloyed or scrap metal as exact ore source;
5. contamination marker as intentional recipe;
6. cluster membership as route or market without mixture model;
7. outlier as foreign import without repair/reuse/recycling audit;
8. mixture scenario as solved economy, scarcity, collapse, or technological
   system without independent evidence.

## Cross-Case Test

| Test | Metal provenance and circulation | Late Roman glass recycling | Provenance-method critiques |
|---|---|---|---|
| Source condition | Geological provenance, old metal circulation, and compositional change overlap. | Antimony/manganese mixing and transition-metal contamination can indicate recycling. | Scientific provenance assumptions can fail when artefacts mix sources or histories. |
| Evidence pressure | Metal composition becomes exact ore or trade route. | Glass group becomes clean primary production zone. | Provenance method becomes source certainty. |
| Core rule | Primary source, secondary circulation, mixture proportion, and inference scale must stay separate. | Recycling markers need probability gates. | Provenance claims need explicit assumptions and alternatives. |

## Automatic Cap

A paper cannot score above `34` if it treats mixed, recycled, remelted, alloyed,
blended, reused, repaired, refired, reworked, contaminated, batched,
composited, cullet-bearing, scrap-bearing, or conservation-affected material as
a single source, direct workshop, exact ore, direct trade route, complete
recipe, intentional technology, foreign import, market map, scarcity proof,
collapse evidence, solved circulation system, or complete industry history
without this checklist or an equivalent recycling/mixture probability gate.

## Source Pressure

- Archaeometallurgical provenance studies foreground old metal circulation,
  mixing, alloying, and compositional change as obstacles to single-source
  claims.
- Late Roman and medieval glass studies show that recycling markers and mixed
  antimony/manganese systems can preserve both primary production and secondary
  reuse signals.
- Recent provenance-method critiques foreground that chemical source matching
  depends on assumptions that fail when artefacts mix raw materials, recipes,
  repairs, and circulation histories.
