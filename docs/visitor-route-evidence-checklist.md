# Visitor Route Evidence Checklist

Use this checklist when a ruin, archaeological park, monument, cave, museumized
site, trail, visitor centre, boardwalk, viewing platform, timed-entry path,
barrier, sign system, lighting plan, queue, guide route, digital tour, or
interpretive path is used as evidence for site access, visibility, circulation,
experience, ritual movement, conservation status, or public memory.

## Required Separations

| Layer | Required custody question |
|---|---|
| Original access | What ancient, historic, or use-period entrances, paths, thresholds, blocked areas, or circulation traces are directly evidenced? |
| Excavated exposure | Which paths or views exist because excavation, removal, reconstruction, sheltering, or conservation made them visible? |
| Modern route | What visitor path, barrier, platform, ramp, stair, queue, one-way loop, closure, or timed route shapes access now? |
| Management purpose | Is the route for conservation, safety, crowd control, accessibility, revenue, interpretation, ritual continuity, or emergency evacuation? |
| View frame | What lighting, railing, platform height, sign placement, replica, window, or camera point controls what visitors see? |
| Wear and impact | What erosion, crowding, touching, dust, humidity, vibration, litter, or maintenance record documents visitor effect? |
| Interpretation layer | Which guide script, panel, app, audio route, map, reconstruction, or exhibit narrative frames the visit? |
| Accessibility | What mobility, sensory, language, ticketing, digital, or replica access substitutes for direct route access? |
| Visitor data | Are counts, flows, dwell time, heat maps, surveys, social media, or carrying-capacity estimates separated from ancient use? |
| Conservation status | Does the route indicate protection needs, damage state, management policy, or original site organization? |
| Public memory | Are "walking ancient footsteps," "authentic route," "complete visit," and pilgrimage/tourism stories contained? |

## Mandatory Refusals

A RUINS paper must refuse:

1. modern visitor route as original circulation;
2. viewing platform as ancient intended viewpoint;
3. barrier or closure as original boundary;
4. interpretive sequence as ritual or historical sequence;
5. visitor flow as ancient traffic, population, or social organization;
6. accessible substitute as direct original access;
7. Instagram/social media route as archaeological significance;
8. current visitor experience as complete site, original experience, or final
   conservation status.

## Cross-Case Test

| Test | Visitor-flow management | Archaeological tourism guidance | Interpretive planning |
|---|---|---|---|
| Source condition | Routes and flows are managed to reduce impacts and improve benefits. | Tourism guidance separates protection, management, guides, operators, and visitor enjoyment. | Planning asks what experiences are desired, available, restricted, or redirected. |
| Evidence pressure | Flow map becomes site organization. | Tour route becomes authentic archaeology. | Interpretive sequence becomes historical sequence. |
| Core rule | Visitor routes are managed access layers, not original circulation or complete site experience. | Visitor use must be separated from conservation, interpretation, and evidence. | Desired experience is not original experience. |

## Automatic Cap

A paper cannot score above `34` if it treats a visitor path, boardwalk,
platform, barrier, closure, queue, timed-entry route, guide script, sign,
lighting plan, visitor centre, digital tour, social media route, crowd-flow
model, carrying-capacity estimate, accessibility substitute, or interpretive
sequence as original circulation, ancient intended viewpoint, original
boundary, ritual sequence, population movement, direct original access,
archaeological significance, complete site experience, original experience, or
final conservation status without this checklist or an equivalent visitor-route
evidence gate.

## Source Pressure

- Visitor-flow management work foregrounds modern routing as a conservation and
  benefit-management process for touristified archaeological sites.
- Archaeological tourism guidance treats visitors, tour operators, guides, site
  managers, protection, funding, and enjoyment as separate management layers.
- Interpretive planning guidance foregrounds desired visitor experiences,
  restrictions, resource protection, accessibility, and interpretation as
  current planning choices.
