# Abu Hureyra Dung Proxies and Animal-Tending Custody

The Abu Hureyra dung-spherulite paper is a strong RUINS trigger because a
microscopic proxy in curated flotation samples is used to reason about animal
tending before and during the shift toward Neolithic herding. The PLOS ONE
study reports dung spherulites in Epipalaeolithic and Neolithic samples and
uses their changing concentrations to discuss on-site animal presence, dung
fuel, plaster preparation, and later shifts in herd management.

The evidence is powerful because it can detect human-animal proximity where
other traces may be sparse. It is also fragile: dung spherulites can be moved,
burned, dissolved, concentrated, curated, counted, and reused in several
activity contexts before becoming an archaeological argument.

RUINS promotes archaeological dung proxy custody to Core here. Dung proxies can
support bounded claims about animal presence, fuel, plaster, and activity
patterns, but they cannot become direct domestication, exact herd location,
complete penning system, full fuel economy, final foddering regime, whole
vegetation history, complete land use, or origin-of-herding story without
separate custody.
