# Amesbury Archer and Isotopic Mobility Custody

The Amesbury Archer is an unlike second test for isotopic mobility custody. The
Egtved Girl case used strontium, hair, nail, and wool to produce a debated
life-story narrative. The Archer case leans on oxygen isotope analysis of tooth
enamel, compared with climate/geographic maps, to suggest that he grew up in a
colder region than Britain, probably central Europe or the Alpine region.

Wessex Archaeology records that the Archer's grave contained an exceptional
assemblage, and that oxygen isotope analysis points to a considerable-distance
origin. A later revisiting keeps the same broad interpretation. British isotope
baseline summaries show why the inference must remain probabilistic: isotope
maps and enamel values constrain possible regions; they do not name an exact
birthplace or route.

RUINS promotes isotopic mobility reconstruction custody to Core here. The
Archer can support a non-local childhood-origin hypothesis and a wider
discussion of Beaker mobility, but isotope evidence cannot become "King of
Stonehenge," exact Alpine biography, metalworking identity, ethnic label, or
complete migration event without separate evidence.
