# Ancestral Pueblo Fingerprints and Biometric Trace Custody

The 2019 PNAS Ancestral Pueblo fingerprint paper is a strong trigger for a new
RUINS capability because it starts from a direct human trace but quickly reaches
social interpretation. Fingerprint impressions on pottery can be measured
through ridge density and compared to biometric reference data, supporting broad
claims about the sex composition of potters in a Chaco-period community.

That evidence is valuable, but it is not self-explaining. A print is a partial
trace on a material substrate. It was shaped by clay behavior, vessel forming,
surface finishing, firing, shrinkage, preservation, recovery, imaging,
measurement, reference-population choice, and statistical uncertainty.

RUINS promotes biometric trace reconstruction custody to Core here. A
fingerprint may support a bounded demographic tendency, but it cannot become an
exact maker, certain sex, precise age, complete apprenticeship story, workforce
census, gender system, or total labor organization without separate custody.
