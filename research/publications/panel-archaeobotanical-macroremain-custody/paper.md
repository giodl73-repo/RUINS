# Archaeobotanical Macroremain Custody

Plant macroremains can make ancient food, farming, storage, and crop processing
visible. Charred seeds, cereal grains, chaff, rachis fragments, weed seeds,
fruits, nutshells, pulses, waterlogged remains, desiccated remains, and
flotation samples can all carry strong archaeological signal.

That signal is not a complete agricultural economy. A seed count, crop ratio,
ubiquity value, density value, immature grain ratio, or chaff assemblage can
support bounded interpretation, but it cannot by itself become exact crop
inventory, complete diet, direct domestication proof, final crop-processing
stage, full storage system, harvest calendar, field ecology, household function,
market supply, famine proof, migration route, ritual meaning, or complete
agricultural economy.

The controlling RUINS move is to separate sample, recovery method, preservation
pathway, context, taxon assignment, crop/weed status, taphonomy, processing
stage, quantification, spatial distribution, seasonality, activity inference,
economy synthesis, and public plant-food memory. Charred-seed taphonomy,
crop-processing models, immature-grain experiments, and archaeobotanical
datasets all point to the same rule: macroremains can anchor plant-use claims
only when recovery, preservation, and inference scale are visible.

This paper promotes `ruins-capability:archaeobotanical-macroremain-custody` to
Core.
