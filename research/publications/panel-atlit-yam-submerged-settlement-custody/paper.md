# Atlit-Yam and the Custody of In-Situ Submerged Settlement Evidence

Atlit-Yam is not Doggerland repeated at smaller scale. Doggerland tests how
RUINS handles a drowned landscape reconstructed through broad proxies.
Atlit-Yam tests the opposite problem: what happens when underwater evidence is
strong enough to look complete.

The responsible claim is that Atlit-Yam preserves substantial in-situ evidence
for a Neolithic coastal settlement now underwater off the Carmel coast. Israel
Antiquities Authority reporting describes underwater excavation and surveys,
places the site hundreds of meters offshore and several meters below sea level,
and lists stone-lined wells, human graves, rectangular stone buildings,
megalithic features, tools, animal remains, fish, plant remains, and food
processing objects. Research summaries describe the site as a well-preserved
submerged prehistoric settlement with material culture and organic remains
characteristic of a mixed farming and fishing economy.

That is unusually strong survival. It still is not total recovery. A well can
anchor water-use and sea-level questions without proving the whole settlement's
social order. A burial can anchor in-situ mortuary evidence without becoming
biography, ancestry, or population identity. Organic and faunal remains can
support subsistence claims while still requiring sample, recovery, and method
custody. A megalithic feature can be recorded as a feature without becoming
ritual certainty in RUINS scope.

The loss process also needs care. Atlit-Yam is evidence for a coastal settlement
later submerged by sea-level change, exposed to waves and currents, and studied
through underwater survey and excavation. It should not become a single disaster
story. Nor should modern climate relevance turn the Neolithic case into a
simple policy parable. RUINS can preserve the analogy as cultural memory while
refusing it as original-state evidence.

Atlit-Yam promotes submerged-landscape proxy custody to Core because it proves
the method works across unlike evidence conditions. Doggerland shows how proxy
maps, cores, models, and trawled finds must not become a complete lost world.
Atlit-Yam shows that even direct in-situ submerged settlement remains must not
become complete settlement history, culture identity, disease history, ritual
meaning, demographic story, or climate event narrative without separate
custody.
