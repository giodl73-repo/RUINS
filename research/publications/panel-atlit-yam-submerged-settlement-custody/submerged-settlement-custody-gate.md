# Submerged Settlement Custody Gate

## Gate Rule

In-situ underwater preservation strengthens a claim, but it does not remove the
need to separate feature, context, sample, interpretation, model, and memory.

## Evidence Layers

| Layer | Can support | Cannot support alone |
|---|---|---|
| Underwater excavation record | Documented feature context. | Complete settlement plan. |
| Wells | Water use, construction, sea-level and salinity questions. | Social organization or abandonment cause. |
| Buildings/installations | Architecture and activity areas where documented. | Whole-village layout or household system. |
| Human graves | Mortuary presence and osteological source questions. | Biography, ancestry, disease history, or population identity. |
| Megalithic features | Recorded feature and possible interpretive question. | Ritual certainty. |
| Tools and food-processing objects | Activity and technology surfaces. | Complete economy or craft system. |
| Faunal/floral remains | Subsistence and environment when method-locked. | Total diet or regional ecology. |
| Sea-level context | Submergence process and coastal change. | Single catastrophe or climate parable. |
| Public image | Reception and science communication. | Original-state proof. |

## Publication Test

Before publication, each Atlit-Yam claim must answer:

1. Is the claim feature-level, site-level, regional, or public-memory?
2. Is the evidence in situ, exposed, re-exposed, sampled, modeled, or inferred?
3. Does the claim require medical, environmental, geographic, or identity
   handoff?
4. Is the exact technical claim being made, refused, or demoted to P3?
