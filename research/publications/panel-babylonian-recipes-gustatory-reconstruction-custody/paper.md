# Babylonian Recipes and Text-Driven Taste Reconstruction

The Yale Babylonian cooking tablets are an unlike second test for gustatory
reconstruction custody. Midas Touch starts from vessel residue and modern
brewing. The Babylonian recipes start from written recipe tablets, translation,
and experimental cooking. Both cases create modern tastes from partial ancient
evidence.

Yale's Babylonian Cooking project presents modern recreations of Babylonian
cooking and offers public recipes such as lamb stew with beets and "Unwinding."
Yale News describes the recipes as impressed into clay tablets and says the
Yale-Harvard team tested the ancient recipes. Harvard's Ancient Recipes project
states that the world's oldest known culinary recipes are preserved on nearly
4,000-year-old clay tablets in the Yale Babylonian Collection, dated to 1730
BCE, and that the project translated the texts and used modern techniques to
reconstruct the recipes.

That evidence supports a strong gustatory-reconstruction claim: these are
source-controlled reconstructions from textual recipe witnesses. It does not
support exact ancient taste. The tablets are terse, damaged, translated, and
missing many modern recipe controls. Ingredient terms, quantities, timing,
temperature, cooking vessels, fuel, water, animal breeds, plant varieties, and
palate all remain separate layers.

The public recipe layer needs discipline. A Yale public recipe can be a valuable
access tool without becoming a safe, exact, or original Babylonian dish. "Oldest
recipe" memory makes the tablets vivid, but RUINS keeps tablet witness,
translation, experimental kitchen, public recipe, and ancient meal apart.
