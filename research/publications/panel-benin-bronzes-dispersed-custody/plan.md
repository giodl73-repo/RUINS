# Plan: Benin Bronzes Dispersed Custody

**Module:** RUINS
**Track:** Monumental Sites and Contested Interpretation
**Status:** publication-ready
**Position in chain:** first dispersed-object provenance custody case

## Research Question

How should RUINS handle a heritage corpus whose objects materially survive, but
whose ruin condition lies in violent removal, dispersal across collections,
museum provenance, partial returns, digital reconnection, and contested public
memory?

## Core Argument

The Benin Bronzes are not a simple lost-object case. Many objects survive, and
some are heavily documented. The absence is relational and custodial: objects
were removed from palace, shrine, ritual, dynastic, and Edo knowledge contexts
in the 1897 British expedition, then dispersed through state, museum, dealer,
auction, donor, and private pathways. RUINS treats the case as dispersed-object
provenance custody: survival of an object does not restore original context,
and return of one object does not settle the status of the whole dispersed
corpus.

## Required Boundaries

1. Keep object identity, material, provenance, current holding, legal title,
   physical return, loan, digital record, and living cultural relation separate.
2. Do not let one museum's return decision settle every object's status.
3. Do not use "Benin Bronzes" as a material-only label; it also covers ivory,
   wood, coral/red stone, regalia, plaques, heads, and related court objects in
   many sources.
4. Do not infer original ritual use or named ownership for every object without
   object-level custody.
5. Hand law, policy, Edo history, object identity, material analysis, and
   geography to sibling systems.

## Capability Decision

This paper creates and activates
`ruins-capability:dispersed-object-provenance-custody`. Promote it to Core only
after a second dispersed-object case tests the same controls outside Benin.
