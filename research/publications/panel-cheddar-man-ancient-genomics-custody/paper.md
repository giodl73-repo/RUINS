# Cheddar Man and Ancient Genomics Reconstruction Custody

Cheddar Man is a strong RUINS pressure case because ancient DNA turned a
Mesolithic skeleton into a public image. The Natural History Museum describes
Cheddar Man as a Mesolithic hunter-gatherer from Gough's Cave, with DNA-based
interpretations that include dark skin, blue eyes, stature, and age-at-death.
The Nature Ecology & Evolution paper assembled genome-wide data from Mesolithic
and Neolithic individuals in Britain, including Cheddar Man, to study population
replacement and hunter-gatherer affinities.

The same case shows why RUINS needs ancient genomics custody. Genome-wide data
can support population-level inference and phenotype probabilities, but public
coverage often compresses that into direct appearance, racial category,
"first Briton" identity, or national ancestry. Facial reconstruction adds
another layer: it is a communication and interpretation product, not a recovered
person.

RUINS therefore activates ancient genomics reconstruction custody. Cheddar Man
can anchor claims about an analysed Mesolithic genome, Western European
hunter-gatherer affinity, and probable phenotype markers. It cannot become exact
face, modern race, direct ancestor, complete British origin story, or total
Mesolithic population history without separate custody.
