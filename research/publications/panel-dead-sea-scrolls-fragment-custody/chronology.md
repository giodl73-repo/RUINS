# Chronology: Dead Sea Scrolls Fragment Custody

**Owner:** STRATA
**Status:** first-pass work surface

## Layered Chronology

| Period | Layer | Evidence condition | RUINS caution |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ancient composition and copying | Texts are composed, copied, used, and deposited before modern recovery. | direct/copy textual through fragments | Do not collapse all texts into one community, genre, or purpose. |
| Deposition and survival | Scrolls fragment, decay, remain hidden, or are preserved unevenly. | direct material / absent | Survival is material and uneven. |
| Modern discovery and acquisition | Fragments enter archaeological, official, private, and market custody. | reported / custody record | Provenance must be source-specific. |
| Photography, edition, and publication | Images, transcriptions, editions, translations, and identifiers proliferate. | mediated / edited textual | Edition is not fragment; translation is not transcription. |
| Reconstruction and joining | Scholars propose joins, columns, lacunae, and manuscript relationships. | reconstruction | Mark supplied text and confidence. |
| Authenticity disputes | Some modern-market fragments receive material/authenticity scrutiny. | material analysis / dispute record | Fragment-set specific, not corpus-wide. |
| Public afterlife | Scrolls become scripture, secrecy, heritage, museum, and authenticity symbols. | cultural memory | Later memory is evidence of reception. |

## Process Categories

| Process | Use in this paper |
|---|---|
| Fragmentation | Physical text survives in partial pieces. |
| Mediation | Photographs, editions, and translations create useful but non-identical evidence surfaces. |
| Reconstruction | Joins and lacunae complete missing structure under uncertainty. |
| Custody drift | Fragment identities and provenance can change across collections and editions. |
| Authenticity pressure | Modern market material needs separate evaluation. |
