# Source Lock: Dead Sea Scrolls Fragment Custody

**Owner:** PALIMPSEST
**Status:** first source lock
**Date:** 2026-07-05

This source lock defines what RUINS may say about Dead Sea Scrolls fragment
custody in this paper. It separates physical fragments, digital images,
metadata, editions, translations, reconstructions, discovery/publication
history, and modern-market authenticity reports.

## Fragment-Custody Ladder

| Custody rung | What it can support | What it cannot support alone |
|---|---|---|
| Physical fragment | Material survival, material analysis, conservation condition, and fragment-specific authenticity claims. | Full manuscript text, translation, or unstated provenance. |
| Official image / spectral image | Visual inspection, visible traces, image-based comparison, and public access claims. | Complete physical custody, all readings, or hidden text without method. |
| Metadata / identifier | Source, shelfmark, cave/manuscript/fragment label, image relation, and navigation. | Stable identity across all scholarly systems without CANON/FONTES audit. |
| Scholarly edition / transcription | Edited readings, line/column conventions, supplied text markers, and bibliography. | Direct material survival unless tied back to fragment/image custody. |
| Translation | Reader-facing interpretation in a modern language. | Ancient wording or surviving text. |
| Reconstruction / join / lacuna | Hypothesis about missing or connected text. | Observed survival unless explicitly constrained and marked. |
| Authenticity report | Tested-fragment/set determination. | Whole-corpus authenticity or provenance beyond tested material. |
| Museum/public narrative | Reception, education, heritage, secrecy, or scandal memory. | Original-state evidence. |

## Paper Use Rules

1. Always identify whether a claim rests on fragment, image, metadata, edition,
   translation, reconstruction, authenticity report, or public narrative.
2. Do not treat translation as surviving text.
3. Do not treat reconstructed lacunae, joins, or column order as direct survival.
4. Do not infer stable provenance from a display label or identifier alone.
5. Do not generalize modern-market authenticity findings to all scrolls.
6. Do not cite official public pages for edition-level readings.
7. Do not provide site-location, collection-acquisition, or handling guidance.
