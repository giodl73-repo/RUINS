# Doggerland and the Custody of Submerged Landscapes

Doggerland is not a lost city. It is a drowned landscape whose evidentiary
condition makes it easy to overbuild a story from scattered proxies.

The responsible RUINS claim is narrow: parts of the southern North Sea preserve
evidence for former terrestrial environments and changing postglacial
conditions. Researchers use geophysical survey, sediment cores, palaeoecology,
trawled finds, faunal remains, ancient environmental DNA, and inundation models
to reconstruct aspects of that landscape. Those evidence streams do not merge
into a complete settlement map or a named culture.

The loss process was not a single cinematic disappearance. Sea-level rise,
coastal change, storm events, sediment movement, and later seabed disturbance
changed both the landscape and the evidence available for study. Some evidence
is basin-scale and modeled. Some is recovered out of original context. Some is
sample-specific. The paper therefore treats Doggerland as a proxy-custody case,
not as a direct excavation case.

This distinction matters because public language often turns Doggerland into a
"lost world." That phrase can be useful as memory of public fascination, but it
cannot do evidentiary work. A submerged palaeolandscape can support claims
about changing environments and possible human use. It cannot, without further
locked evidence, support precise routes, villages, social identities, named
peoples, or a civilizational fall story.

The strongest RUINS result is a refusal discipline:

- seabed form is not settlement layout;
- seismic interpretation is not occupation proof;
- a trawled artefact is not an in-situ context;
- a sediment core is not a regional population history;
- ancient environmental DNA is not a named group;
- inundation modeling is not a lived event narrative;
- public lost-world language is not original-state evidence.

Doggerland belongs in RUINS because it demonstrates how absence can be mapped
without being domesticated into a complete story. The project can say that a
landscape existed, changed, was used by humans in some places, and was later
submerged. It cannot responsibly turn that into a recoverable country, route,
or mythic ruin.
