# Dresden Frauenkirche and Monument-Scale Archival Reconstruction

Dresden's Frauenkirche is a RUINS case where a collapsed monument was rebuilt
from rubble, surviving fabric, historical records, modeling, modern engineering,
and public memory. The case is not simply "the church returned." It is a layered
evidence problem: what survived, what was recovered, what was documented, what
was modeled, what was newly made, and what later memory asked the rebuilt church
to mean.

The Frauenkirche Dresden Foundation describes the postwar state as a mountain of
rubble and records reconstruction beginning in 1994, with completion in 2005.
Its chronology names 1993 removal of ruins plus mapping, recovery, and
cataloguing of finds. Its technical account says the rebuilding used historical
building fabric, original historical plans, modern structural engineering,
historical plans and floor plans, photogrammetric recording of the ruins, and a
computer-aided three-dimensional model.

That evidence supports a strong reconstruction-custody claim: Dresden is a
stone-by-stone archival reconstruction case. It does not support a total
original-fabric claim. Reused stones and historical plans improve custody, but
new sandstone, revised structural calculations, corrected load paths, modern
mortar specifications, rebuilt interior work, and a new social mission remain
separate layers.

The memory layer is unusually strong. The Foundation frames the reconstruction
as donation-funded, nationally and internationally supported, and a sign of East
and West German togetherness, reconciliation, and European unity. The support
society page preserves the earlier ruin as a memorial and the appeal to rebuild
the church as a Christian world peace center. UNESCO's Dresden document places
the Frauenkirche inside the broader postwar restoration and reconstruction of
Dresden, but also notes that Dresden's policy differed from Warsaw's by using a
critical selection of significant and well documented buildings.

RUINS therefore treats the rebuilt Frauenkirche as source-controlled and
publication-ready evidence for archival reconstruction custody. It refuses to
treat the rebuilt monument as complete original fabric, exact historical
interior, direct continuity, final conservation doctrine, or total recovery from
wartime destruction.
