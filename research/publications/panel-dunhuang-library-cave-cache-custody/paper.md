# Dunhuang Cave 17 and the Custody of Sealed Cache Survival

Dunhuang Cave 17 is famous as the "Library Cave." RUINS should treat that name
as a useful public handle, not as a solved evidentiary claim.

The responsible claim is strong but bounded. International Dunhuang Programme
materials describe Cave 17 as a chamber sealed for centuries that contained
more than 50,000 manuscripts, paintings, printed documents, textiles, and other
objects dating from the 4th to 11th centuries. The same guidance records a
modern discovery sequence around Wang Yuanlu, who became custodian of the
materials and later sold or distributed bundles to foreign scholars and
archaeologists, including Aurel Stein.

That survival is extraordinary. It still does not make the cache a complete
library. A sealed room can preserve a mass of material while leaving the
deposition purpose uncertain. A bundle can preserve a local grouping while not
proving the order of the whole chamber. A catalogue record can identify a
surviving item while not restoring its original institutional setting. A
digitized image can widen access while not undoing physical dispersal or
settling the meaning of the original cache.

Recent scholarship on the Cave 17 bundles makes the RUINS boundary sharper:
there is evidence that Stein's documentation can help reconstruct some bundle
contents as he encountered them, but not the full order of the cave itself.
The same scholarship stresses that the original purpose of the cache has been
variously explained and remains contested. RUINS therefore treats Cave 17 as a
sealed-cache custody case, not as a complete medieval library recovered intact.

The strongest publication result is a refusal discipline:

- sealed survival is not complete representativeness;
- cache volume is not a whole institutional inventory;
- item diversity is not whole Silk Road history;
- expedition site marks are not original cave order;
- dispersal records are not neutral provenance;
- digitization is not physical reunification;
- "secret library" language is cultural memory, not original function.

Dunhuang belongs in RUINS because it shows that abundance can mislead as easily
as absence. The cave did not only lose material. It also lost original context,
order, motive, custody continuity, and institutional wholeness. Responsible
reconstruction begins by keeping those losses visible.
