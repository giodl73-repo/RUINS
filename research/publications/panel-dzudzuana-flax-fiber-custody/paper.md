# Dzudzuana Flax Fibers and Perishable Technology Custody

The Dzudzuana Cave flax-fiber paper is a strong RUINS trigger because it uses
microscopic perishable traces to infer technologies that rarely survive as
complete objects. The Science paper reports wild flax fibers from Upper
Paleolithic layers at Dzudzuana Cave in Georgia, including spun or twisted
fibers, colored fibers, cut ends, and related microtraces.

The evidence matters because it opens a window onto cordage, basketry, sewing,
hafting, skin/fur work, and cloth-related possibilities before preserved
textiles are available. It is also fragile: a fiber is not a garment, a twist is
not a whole cordage system, and a colored fiber is not complete dyeing
technology.

RUINS promotes perishable fiber microtrace custody to Core here. Microscopic
fibers can support bounded perishable-technology inference, but they cannot
become complete textiles, exact garments, woven cloth, full baskets, hafting
systems, craft specialization, fashion history, trade networks, or
oldest-clothing stories without separate custody.
