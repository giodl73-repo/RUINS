# Egtved Girl and Isotopic Mobility Reconstruction Custody

The Egtved Girl is a RUINS pressure case because a chemically small evidence
surface became a large public biography. The 2015 Scientific Reports paper used
strontium isotope analysis of tooth enamel, hair, nail, textiles, and associated
materials to reconstruct origin, mobility, diet, and wool provenance for a
well-preserved Bronze Age burial.

The same case also shows why RUINS needs custody. Public summaries quickly turn
isotope mismatch into "not Danish" or "from the Black Forest" claims, while
later research and commentary raise problems around local baselines, liming,
Scandinavian alternatives, and the difference between personal movement and
textile provenance.

RUINS therefore activates isotopic mobility reconstruction custody. The paper
may say that isotope values constrain local versus non-local possibilities and
support mobility hypotheses. It may not turn a baseline comparison into exact
birthplace, ethnicity, route, biography, or national-origin story.
