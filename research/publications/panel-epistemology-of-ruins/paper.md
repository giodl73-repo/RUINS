# The Epistemology of Ruins

**Authors:** draft
**Target Venue:** portfolio research note before external venue targeting
**Status:** working draft
**Date:** 2026-07-04

## Abstract

RUINS studies cases where knowledge survives unevenly: damaged artifacts,
fragmentary texts, abandoned settlements, destroyed archives, lost techniques,
and contested reconstructions. This paper proposes the first RUINS method. It
argues that a ruin is not merely a remnant, but a structured relation between
absence, trace, inference, reconstruction, and later memory. The method defines
evidence conditions, claim grades, reconstruction-status labels, and a required
refusal section. It then tests the vocabulary against five unlike cases: Indus
script, Antikythera mechanism, Library of Alexandria, Cahokia, and Roman
concrete.

## 1. Problem

Ruins invite overconfident stories. A wall becomes a palace. A burn layer becomes
an invasion. A fragment becomes a lost book. A replica becomes a recovery. A
famous loss becomes a symbol stronger than the evidence behind it.

The problem is not that reconstruction is wrong. Reconstruction is often the
only way to think with damaged evidence. The problem is unmarked reconstruction:
claims that move from trace to story without showing where inference entered.

## 2. Working Definition

A ruin is a partial-survival condition in which an object, site, text,
institution, technique, or cultural system remains knowable only through an
uneven record.

## 3. Claim Grades

| Grade | Use |
|---|---|
| Observed | Directly attested by surviving trace or source custody. |
| Corroborated | Supported by multiple independent traces or source lines. |
| Plausible | Fits the available evidence while admitting meaningful alternatives. |
| Speculative | Possible, but underdetermined by the surviving record. |
| Legendary | Important to cultural memory, but not reliable as direct evidence. |
| Modern projection | A later restoration, visualization, or story that may be useful but must not be confused with the lost original. |

## 4. Refusal Discipline

RUINS should treat refusal as a positive scholarly act. Refusal prevents the
project from turning every absence into a completed story.

Useful refusals include:

- The site plan does not prove the political structure.
- The surviving fragment does not prove the whole text.
- A later copy does not prove the original wording.
- A working replica does not prove ancient users understood it in the same way.
- A famous destruction story does not prove a single dramatic loss event.

## 5. Conclusion

RUINS should not begin by building a database. It should begin by proving that
its method can keep five unlike cases honest.
