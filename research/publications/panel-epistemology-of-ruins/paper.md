# The Epistemology of Ruins

**Authors:** draft
**Target Venue:** portfolio research note before external venue targeting
**Status:** publication-ready
**Date:** 2026-07-04

## Abstract

RUINS studies cases where knowledge survives unevenly: damaged artifacts,
fragmentary texts, abandoned settlements, destroyed archives, lost techniques,
and contested reconstructions. This paper proposes the first RUINS method. It
argues that a ruin is not merely a remnant, but a structured relation between
absence, trace, inference, reconstruction, and later memory. The method defines
evidence conditions, claim grades, reconstruction-status labels, and a required
refusal section. It then tests the vocabulary against unlike cases: Indus
script, Antikythera mechanism, Library of Alexandria, Cahokia, Roman concrete,
Knossos, and Great Zimbabwe.

## 1. Problem

Ruins invite overconfident stories. A wall becomes a palace. A burn layer becomes
an invasion. A fragment becomes a lost book. A replica becomes a recovery. A
famous loss becomes a symbol stronger than the evidence behind it.

The problem is not that reconstruction is wrong. Reconstruction is often the
only way to think with damaged evidence. The problem is unmarked reconstruction:
claims that move from trace to story without showing where inference entered.

The current method claims are locked in [`method-lock.md`](method-lock.md) and
validated against the completed case set in
[`case-validation.md`](case-validation.md).

## 2. Working Definition

A ruin is a partial-survival condition in which an object, site, text,
institution, technique, or cultural system remains knowable only through an
uneven record.

This definition includes physical ruins, but it is wider than architecture. It
can include a destroyed library, an undeciphered script, a damaged machine, a
lost workshop process, a vanished city, a broken lineage archive, or a restored
site whose modern restoration now shapes the object being studied.

## 3. Evidence Conditions

| Condition | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Direct material | The object, structure, trace, or fragment survives physically. |
| Direct textual | A contemporary or near-contemporary text survives. |
| Copied textual | A later copy, quotation, translation, inventory, or paraphrase survives. |
| Contextual | The surrounding settlement, layer, institution, or practice survives better than the focal object. |
| Reported | A witness or later source reports the object or loss. |
| Reconstructed | A modern restoration, model, replica, visualization, or operational hypothesis supplies missing structure. |
| Absent | The relevant evidence is missing, destroyed, inaccessible, or not yet found. |

These conditions can overlap. A damaged machine may have direct material
survival and reconstructed operation. A lost library may have reported loss,
copied textual survival, and strong cultural memory but weak direct evidence for
a single destruction event.

## 4. Claim Grades

| Grade | Use |
|---|---|
| Observed | Directly attested by surviving trace or source custody. |
| Corroborated | Supported by multiple independent traces or source lines. |
| Plausible | Fits the available evidence while admitting meaningful alternatives. |
| Speculative | Possible, but underdetermined by the surviving record. |
| Legendary | Important to cultural memory, but not reliable as direct evidence. |
| Modern projection | A later restoration, visualization, or story that may be useful but must not be confused with the lost original. |

The grade is not a decoration. It should force the writer to show why a claim is
being made at that strength.

## 5. Reconstruction Status

Reconstruction is not one thing. RUINS should distinguish at least five statuses:

| Status | Meaning |
|---|---|
| Minimal completion | Missing parts are supplied only where directly constrained by surviving evidence. |
| Functional model | A model works mechanically or logically, but more than one completion may fit the evidence. |
| Visual restoration | A modern image or built restoration supplies appearance, often with aesthetic choices. |
| Scholarly hypothesis | A reconstruction is argued in the literature but remains debated. |
| Public image | A reconstruction has become culturally familiar regardless of its evidence basis. |

## 6. Required Case Sections

Every RUINS case paper should include:

1. What is missing
2. What survives
3. How the loss happened or accumulated
4. Evidence-condition table
5. Claim-grade table
6. Reconstruction-status table
7. Contested claims
8. Cultural-memory afterlife
9. Refusals
10. Sibling Knowledge Systems handoffs

## 7. Stress Tests

### 7.1 Indus Script

The Indus script is a test of non-decipherment. RUINS should be able to describe
the surviving inscription corpus, sign patterns, artifact contexts, and proposed
interpretations without endorsing a decipherment. The refusal section matters
most: a responsible paper must say that sign recurrence is not the same as
reading.

### 7.2 Antikythera Mechanism

The Antikythera mechanism tests damaged artifact reconstruction. Surviving gears
and inscriptions constrain real functions, but modern models complete missing
parts. RUINS should separate artifact evidence, imaging evidence, gear-train
inference, operational reconstruction, and public wonder.

### 7.3 Library of Alexandria

The Library of Alexandria tests famous loss. The popular story often compresses
multiple institutions, fires, political events, and later memory into one
dramatic destruction. RUINS should ask which library, which collections, which
loss events, which sources, and which later myths.

### 7.4 Cahokia

Cahokia tests abandoned urban systems. RUINS should resist simple collapse
theater. Settlement change, environmental pressure, political transformation,
migration, disease, trade, and ritual change may all matter without producing a
single tidy ending.

### 7.5 Roman Concrete

Roman concrete tests lost technique. A technique may be partially documented,
archaeologically visible, continuously practiced in altered form, chemically
analyzable, and experimentally recoverable. Calling it simply "lost" may hide
the actual transmission problem.

### 7.6 Knossos

Knossos tests restoration as modern projection. The restored palace is not
simply false, but it is also not transparent evidence for Bronze Age appearance.
RUINS should separate excavated material, restoration choices, public image, and
the site's modern afterlife.

### 7.7 Great Zimbabwe

Great Zimbabwe tests interpretive violence. Monumental survival did not prevent
colonial denial from attacking African authorship and distorting public
understanding. RUINS should treat damaged custody, living memory, heritage
recognition, and denial history as separate evidence layers.

## 9. Refusal Discipline

RUINS should treat refusal as a positive scholarly act. Refusal prevents the
project from turning every absence into a completed story.

Useful refusals include:

- The site plan does not prove the political structure.
- The surviving fragment does not prove the whole text.
- A later copy does not prove the original wording.
- A working replica does not prove ancient users understood it in the same way.
- A famous destruction story does not prove a single dramatic loss event.
- A modern restoration can become part of the site's history without becoming
  evidence for the ancient state.

The method-level refusal register is
[`refusal-register.md`](refusal-register.md).

## 10. Conclusion

RUINS should not begin by building a database. It should begin by proving that
its method can keep five unlike cases honest. If the evidence vocabulary
survives Indus script, Antikythera mechanism, Library of Alexandria, Cahokia,
and Roman concrete, then a later schema or runtime may be justified. Until then,
the research-paper format is the right constraint.
