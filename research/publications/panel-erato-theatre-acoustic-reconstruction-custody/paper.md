# ERATO and Virtual Acoustic Restoration of Ancient Theatres

The ERATO project is an unlike second test for acoustic-space reconstruction
custody. Stonehenge uses a physical scale model of a reconstructed stone circle.
ERATO uses computer models and virtual reconstructions of ancient theatres and
odea. Both make lost sound environments accessible, and both can be overread as
direct ancient hearing.

Public records for ERATO describe an aim to investigate open-air theatres and
roofed odea, using computer models of spaces as they exist today, calibrated by
acoustic measurements from well-preserved examples such as Aspendos and Jerash.
The next step was to complete models according to archaeological information and
make virtual reconstructions. The Roman theatres paper says virtual
reconstructions used computer models, and that musical instruments and short
pieces of music were reconstructed and recorded.

That evidence supports a strong acoustic-reconstruction claim: ERATO can compare
types of ancient performance spaces and explore modeled acoustic properties. It
does not support direct ancient hearing. Computer models depend on geometry,
materials, calibration choices, missing roofs/scenery, surface assumptions,
performer position, audience size, reconstructed instruments, and modern
playback.

The public memory layer is attractive: ancient theatres are famous for
"excellent acoustics," and auralization makes them feel revived. RUINS treats
revival as access and method, not as the original performance, original audience
experience, or final proof of theatrical design intention.
