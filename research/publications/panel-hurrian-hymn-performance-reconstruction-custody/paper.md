# Hurrian Hymn H6 and Performance Reconstruction Custody

Hurrian Hymn H6 is a RUINS case where the surviving object is not a building,
body, pigment trace, or archive, but a possible route back to sound. The tablet
preserves song text and musical instructions, yet the performance that modern
listeners want is missing. RUINS therefore treats the case as a custody problem:
which layers are material, which are textual, which are interpretive, and which
are modern performance afterlife?

The National Museum of Damascus virtual museum page identifies the object as a
terracotta tablet from Ras Shamra, ancient Ugarit, dated to about 1400 B.C. It
describes cuneiform musical writing on the lower part of the tablet, separated
from the hymn text by a line. The page says the Akkadian instruction text gives
interval names followed by numbers, but also states that no one is sure how
those numbers should be interpreted or transposed onto modern staff notation.
It explicitly warns that the melody transmitted in clay cannot be played with
certainty.

Recent scholarship sharpens the pressure. Dylan Lawrence Gibson's 2026 article
in Early Music treats H6 as one of the oldest and most complete examples of
instructional music notation in cuneiform, but stresses that the relative
completeness has produced competing interpretations. It argues that modern
Western notation can reduce the ambiguity of the ancient instructions by turning
string-number or interval data into apparently fixed pitch, rhythm, scale, and
performance choices. The proposed alternative, tablaturization, keeps more of
the ancient instruction structure visible.

That evidence supports a strong RUINS claim: H6 is a publication-ready trigger
for notated performance reconstruction custody. It does not support exact melody,
exact rhythm, exact tempo, direct ancient sound, complete ritual performance, or
performance-recording equivalence. A modern playable version may be useful as
interpretation, teaching, or artistic response, but it must not become the
ancient performance itself.

The public memory layer is obvious and risky. "Oldest song" language makes the
tablet legible to broad audiences, but also compresses tablet, notation,
decipherment, edition, instrument, performer, and recording into one claim. RUINS
keeps those layers apart.
