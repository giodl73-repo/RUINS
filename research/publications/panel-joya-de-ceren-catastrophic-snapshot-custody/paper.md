# Joya de Ceren and the Custody of Preserved Village Snapshots

Joya de Ceren is often called the "Pompeii of the Americas." RUINS treats that
phrase as a warning label as much as a public description.

The responsible claim is strong: UNESCO describes Joya de Ceren as a settlement
founded after earlier Ilopango ash had weathered into fertile soil, then
destroyed not long afterward by the eruption of Loma Caldera. UNESCO also
records its 1976 discovery during silo construction, when a bulldozer exposed a
clay-built structure. The site preserves a village context with unusual
archaeological value because volcanic ash protected architecture and activity
surfaces that normally vanish.

That preservation is not total recovery. A household structure can anchor a
specific activity area, not every household relationship. Plant remains and
fields can anchor agricultural evidence, not the entire crop economy. Objects
left in place can support a context claim, not a precise final action. The
absence of bodies can raise an evacuation question, but it cannot alone prove
who escaped, when, or how.

Joya de Ceren promotes catastrophic-snapshot custody to Core because it tests
the Akrotiri gate against a different preservation condition. Akrotiri tempts
urban, artistic, and final-day overclaims. Joya de Ceren tempts domestic and
agricultural overclaims: complete ordinary Maya life, exact last meal, exact
field system, and a whole village frozen in a single instant.

The strongest RUINS result is a refusal discipline:

- ash burial is not total daily life;
- household preservation is not complete household history;
- plant preservation is not complete crop economy;
- object context is not exact final action;
- absent bodies are not escape certainty;
- UNESCO value is not whole-society reconstruction;
- "Pompeii of the Americas" is memory, not method.

Joya de Ceren matters because it gives rare access to ordinary settlement and
agricultural contexts. RUINS preserves that strength by refusing to make the
snapshot more complete than it is.
