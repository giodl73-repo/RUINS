# Kyrenia Reconstruction Context Loss

**Module:** RUINS
**Status:** working draft
**Date:** 2026-07-05

## Abstract

The Kyrenia ship is a RUINS case because unusually strong preservation does
not remove context loss. The wreck gives direct access to hull timbers, cargo,
excavation records, conservation history, reconstruction, replicas, catalog
systems, and revised dating work. Those layers make the case powerful, but they
also create overclaim pressure: reconstructed hull becomes original vessel,
cargo origin becomes route map, dating range becomes voyage story, and museum
or digital display becomes original operating context.

## Why Kyrenia Tests The Capability

Uluburun tested cargo abundance. Kyrenia tests reconstruction abundance. The
problem is not that too little survives. The problem is that survival arrives
through many mediated layers:

- underwater discovery and excavation,
- object and hull recovery,
- conservation treatment,
- reconstructed hull display,
- replica and performance afterlife,
- final report synthesis,
- digital catalog and virtual museum,
- new sample cleaning and radiocarbon/date modeling.

RUINS should treat these as evidence layers, not collapse them into one
complete ancient ship story.

## What Survives

| Surface | Evidence condition | Current grade |
|---|---|---|
| Shipwreck identity | INA and publication-summary custody. | Source-specific |
| Excavation history | Public INA and final-report metadata. | Source-specific |
| Hull preservation | INA and publication-summary custody. | Source-specific, not object-level |
| Cargo classes | Public/source-summary custody. | Broad classes only |
| Conservation and reconstruction | INA/Cornell/source-summary custody. | Modern evidence layer |
| Dating revision | Cornell summary of PLOS ONE work. | Source-specific science summary |
| Museum/digital catalog | Virtual museum public custody. | Modern afterlife and research surface |

## What Does Not Survive Cleanly

The ship's original operating context is not identical to its recovered and
reconstructed state. RUINS separates:

- ancient hull from reconstructed hull,
- cargo from route,
- cargo source from home port,
- date range from voyage narrative,
- replica performance from original performance,
- digital catalog from original object relation,
- museum display from original stowage.

## Claim Grades

| Claim | Grade | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| Kyrenia is an ancient shipwreck excavated near Cyprus in the late 1960s. | Source-specific | INA and final-report metadata support this. |
| A large part of the hull survived and was conserved/reconstructed. | Source-specific | INA supports broad statement; exact timber claims need report custody. |
| Cargo and ceramics support questions about Rhodes and trade. | Qualified | Final-report summary supports cautious framing; no full route map. |
| Replica sailing proves original performance. | Refuse | Replica evidence is modern experiment/reception unless source-locked. |
| Digital catalog equals original context. | Refuse | Catalog is modern research custody. |

## Context-Loss Ladder

1. Wreck event damages operating context.
2. Seabed conditions preserve and displace evidence.
3. Excavation records objects before removal.
4. Conservation changes materials into stable museum/research objects.
5. Reconstruction arranges surviving hull evidence into a modern interpretive
   form.
6. Replicas and digital systems generate new afterlife evidence.
7. Later dating work revises chronology without completing route, owner, or
   voyage narrative.

## Refusals

RUINS refuses the following claims in this draft:

- the reconstructed hull is the ancient ship without qualification;
- cargo origin proves complete route;
- the ship has a settled owner, crew biography, or exact final itinerary;
- museum display or virtual catalog recreates original stowage;
- replica sailing performance proves original ship performance;
- exact amphora, pottery, coin, hull, or object claims without final-report or
  catalogue page custody;
- site-location, diving, recovery, or collecting guidance.

## Conclusion

Kyrenia confirms that shipwreck context loss is not only a problem of missing
evidence. It is also a problem of mediated abundance. The more preservation,
reconstruction, display, and analysis survive, the more RUINS must grade which
layer is ancient evidence and which layer is modern recovery, interpretation,
or afterlife.
