# Laetoli Footprint Trackway Custody

**Status:** publication-ready
**Track:** Evidence and Absence
**Capability:** `ruins-capability:cast-void-identity-custody`

## Abstract

The Laetoli footprints are famous because they make vanished movement visible.
Wet volcanic ash captured early hominin footprints and later ash preserved the
trackways. That apparent immediacy is exactly why RUINS needs custody. A
footprint is not a skeleton, a named individual, a full species diagnosis, or a
complete social scene. It is a negative trace shaped by substrate, preservation,
excavation, cleaning, measurement, casts, photography, scans, comparative
samples, and public origin-story memory.

## Evidence Layers

| Layer | Current claim | Limit |
|---|---|---|
| Substrate | Wet volcanic ash preserved footprint impressions. | Not full geology or dating audit. |
| Site G trackway | Smithsonian records an almost 27 m trail with about 70 early human footprints. | Trail summary, not full catalogue. |
| Taxon attribution | Smithsonian says Au. afarensis is the most likely maker for Site G. | Likely attribution, not individual identity. |
| Gait signal | Prints support bipedal gait features such as heel-strike and toe-off. | Not a complete locomotor model. |
| Site A dispute | Nature records Site A as once marginalized by bear-affinity hypotheses and later re-excavated. | Comparative study, not universal taxon closure. |
| Digital access | Nature used 3D photogrammetry, laser scanning, photographs, and searched-for casts. | Models and casts are mediation layers. |
| Public memory | Laetoli anchors "ancestor footsteps" and early bipedalism narratives. | Memory afterlife, not proof of every social claim. |

## Source-Locked Synthesis

The Smithsonian source supports the public Site G summary: the trail is almost
27 m long, includes about 70 footprints, dates to about 3.6 million years ago,
and is most likely associated with Australopithecus afarensis. It also supports
the basic preservation story: early humans walked through wet volcanic ash and
later ash covered and preserved the traces.

The Nature article gives the second pressure surface. It records Site A as a
nearby bipedal trackway whose attribution was disputed, then re-excavated and
documented with photogrammetry and laser scanning. The study argues that Site A
resembles hominin rather than bear tracks and suggests locomotor diversity at
Laetoli. For RUINS, this does not authorize casual species certainty. It shows
why trace morphology, comparative samples, substrate, cleaning, scans, and
prior interpretations must stay separate.

## What RUINS Refuses

| Overclaim | RUINS response |
|---|---|
| A footprint is a complete individual. | It is a negative trace of contact, not a body. |
| Site G proves every detail of Au. afarensis locomotion. | It supports source-scoped bipedal gait claims. |
| Site A is settled without comparative custody. | Nature's claim depends on re-excavation, cleaning, scans, and comparisons. |
| Track proximity proves family, group intent, or social relation. | Spatial relation is not social identity. |
| Casts, scans, or photos equal the original trackway. | They are access and analysis layers. |
| Laetoli is a complete human-origin story. | It is a powerful trace, not a total origin narrative. |

## Method Result

Laetoli promotes cast-void identity custody to Core. Pompeii showed body voids
becoming identity and tragedy stories. Laetoli shows footprints becoming taxon,
gait, group, and origin stories. The reusable rule is the same: when absence
looks like presence, RUINS must separate the trace from the actor.
