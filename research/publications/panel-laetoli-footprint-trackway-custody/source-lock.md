## Locked Claims

| Claim | Grade | Source posture |
|---|---|---|
| Site G is an almost 27 m trail with about 70 early human footprints. | P2 | Smithsonian supports this. |
| The footprints were preserved after walking through wet volcanic ash and later ash cover. | P2 | Smithsonian supports this preservation summary. |
| Site G footprints are most likely associated with Australopithecus afarensis. | P2 | Smithsonian supports likely attribution, not certainty. |
| Site G gait claims include bipedalism, aligned big toe, heel-strike, and toe-off. | P2 | Smithsonian supports summary-level gait interpretation. |
| Site A was re-excavated, cleaned, and digitally documented with photogrammetry and laser scanning. | P2 | Nature supports this. |
| Site A was compared with bears, chimpanzees, and humans and interpreted as more hominin-like than ursid. | P2 | Nature supports study-specific inference. |
| Laetoli may show locomotor diversity among hominins. | P3 | Allowed only as Nature study conclusion; no total species model. |

## Current Refusals

1. Do not treat footprints as whole individuals.
2. Do not turn likely Au. afarensis attribution into identity certainty.
3. Do not infer family, social group, intention, or emotion from track spacing.
4. Do not treat casts, scans, photos, or models as the original trackway.
5. Do not turn Site A into universal locomotor closure.
6. Do not make exact substrate, dating, or conservation claims without future
   source custody.
