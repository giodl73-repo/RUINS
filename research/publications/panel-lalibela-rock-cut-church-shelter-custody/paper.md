# Lalibela and the Custody of a Sheltered Rock-Hewn Church

Lalibela extends the Petra rule into a different rock-cut condition. Petra is a
half-built, half-carved city whose public image can shrink to a single facade.
Lalibela is a living religious complex where the iconic church image can hide
drainage, trenches, interiors, paintings, temporary shelters, damage, and
pilgrimage use.

UNESCO describes Lalibela as eleven medieval monolithic cave churches, a
thirteenth-century New Jerusalem, and a continuing place of pilgrimage and
devotion. Its own account separates the churches into northern and southern
groups, with Biete Ghiorgis isolated but connected by trenches. It also notes
that the churches were hewn from living rock, then chiselled into doors,
windows, columns, floors, roofs, drainage ditches, trenches, ceremonial passages,
hermit caves, and catacombs. A single photograph of Biete Ghiorgis cannot carry
that whole system.

The conservation problem is equally layered. UNESCO says drainage ditches were
filled for centuries, cleared in the twentieth century, and disrupted by seismic
activity, causing severe water-damage degradation. It records structural concern
at Biet Amanuel, degradation of paintings, damaged sculptures and bas-reliefs,
and temporary lightweight shelters over some churches that protect while also
affecting visual integrity. The site remains a living pilgrimage place managed
through Church/State responsibility, with unresolved planning, buffer, housing,
tourism, and conservation-plan issues.

Lalibela therefore promotes rock-cut facade erosion custody to core RUINS
method. The paper must separate church image, carved surface, interior,
drainage, trench, shelter, mural condition, sculpture condition, living worship,
tourism pressure, and whole religious history. A shelter is not finished
conservation. A church image is not the whole site. Living use is evidence of
continuity, not proof of every original function or construction sequence.

The responsible conclusion is narrow. Lalibela can stand as a second rock-cut
erosion case because the same method controls a different site type: living-rock
churches rather than sandstone facades. RUINS owns the custody rule; religious
meaning, Ethiopian history, conservation science, named church identity,
pilgrimage practice, and tourism governance belong to sibling systems.
