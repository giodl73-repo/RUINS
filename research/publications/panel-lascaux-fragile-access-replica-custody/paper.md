# Lascaux and the Custody of Fragile Access

Lascaux is not simply a cave that can no longer be visited. It is a case where
the public object has split: original cave, conservation object, replica,
virtual tour, and cultural icon.

The responsible RUINS claim is narrow. UNESCO describes the Vézère Valley as a
Palaeolithic landscape with decorated caves, especially Lascaux, whose 1940
discovery was important for the history of prehistoric art. UNESCO highlights
the animal figures for their detail, colour, and vivid rendering. The French
Ministry of Culture presents Lascaux as an inaccessible original whose works can
be encountered through virtual visits and replicas. Lascaux IV describes itself
as the only full and exact replica of the original cave.

Those claims do not merge. A replica can support public access and
interpretation. It cannot become the original cave. A digital tour can support
visual inspection and pedagogy. It cannot settle rock surface, pigment,
microclimate, or conservation status. UNESCO value can anchor importance. It
cannot settle chronology, ritual meaning, or the whole culture that made the
images.

Lascaux is therefore a fragile-access custody case. Its strongest RUINS lesson
is not just "replicas need labels." It is that conservation can make mediation
the main evidence encountered by the public. That mediation must be named every
time a claim moves from original cave to facsimile, digital image, visitor
experience, or cultural memory.

The strongest refusals are:

- public access is not original access;
- replica fidelity is not object equivalence;
- virtual inspection is not conservation measurement;
- animal imagery is not ritual certainty;
- World Heritage value is not complete cultural interpretation;
- "prehistoric masterpiece" language is reception, not method.

Lascaux belongs in RUINS because the surviving site is protected partly by
withdrawing it. Responsible reconstruction has to preserve the difference
between the thing protected and the thing shown.
