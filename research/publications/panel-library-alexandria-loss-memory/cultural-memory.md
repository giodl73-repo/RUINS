# Cultural Memory Register: Library of Alexandria Loss and Memory

**Owner:** RELIQUARY
**Status:** first example register
**Date:** 2026-07-04

This register records how the Library circulates after the loss. These entries
are not evidence for ancient holdings or final destruction. They are evidence
for how an uncertain loss became a durable symbol.

## Examples

| Memory form | Example surface | What it does | RUINS use | Limit |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Single-burning narrative | Public summaries and popular histories often compress the Library's fate into one dramatic fire. | Gives the loss a clean scene, date, and emotional shape. | Shows why RUINS must separate source traditions from memory. | Not evidence for one final destruction event. |
| Villain assignment | Caesar, Christians, or Muslims are blamed in different retellings. | Turns layered loss into moral accusation. | Tracks how blame narratives organize memory. | Requires source layer before historical use. |
| Universal-library myth | The Library is imagined as containing all or nearly all ancient knowledge. | Makes the loss feel like a civilizational break. | Useful as modern symbolic afterlife. | Not evidence for actual holdings. |
| Anti-neglect warning | Modern essays use Alexandria to warn about public libraries, archives, war, censorship, or institutional neglect. | Converts ancient uncertainty into a present-day preservation lesson. | Cultural-memory evidence about why the story persists. | Not ancient evidence. |
| Scholarly caution narrative | Modern scholarship often stresses that the fate was gradual, layered, or source-problematic. | Counters the single-burning myth. | Supports RUINS' refusal discipline. | Does not solve the final fate by itself. |

## Paper Rules

1. Cultural-memory examples may explain why the Library remains emotionally
   powerful.
2. They may not upgrade a destruction claim, holdings claim, or blame claim.
3. When public writing names a villain, RUINS should identify the source layer
   before repeating the claim.
4. When public writing says "all ancient knowledge," RUINS should restate it as
   universal-library symbolism.

## Review Result

For this paper, later memory is treated as afterlife evidence only. None of the
memory examples are used to identify the Library's holdings or final destruction
event.
