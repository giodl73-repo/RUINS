# Lindow Man Preserved Body Custody

**Status:** publication-ready
**Track:** Evidence and Absence
**Capability:** `ruins-capability:preserved-body-taphonomy-custody`

## Abstract

Lindow Man is a preserved bog body, but preservation does not make him
biographically transparent. The British Museum record separates object
identity, body parts, findspot, radiocarbon range, violent-death findings, peat
preservation chemistry, conservation treatment, and display. RUINS treats these
as distinct custody layers. Multiple injuries can support a violent-death
claim, but they do not by themselves settle motive, ritual, social role, or
identity.

## Source-Locked Synthesis

The British Museum record identifies Lindow Man as Lindow II and distinguishes
other remains from Lindow Moss, including Lindow I, III, and IV. It describes
him as a well-preserved peat-bog body found near Manchester in 1984, records a
violent death and radiocarbon range, and explicitly refuses certainty about
whether he was killed as sacrifice, crime victim, or executed criminal.

The same record is crucial for preservation and display custody. Peat bogs are
cold, acidic, low-oxygen environments that can turn soft tissue leathery. After
removal, the British Museum used polyethylene glycol, wrapping, freezing, and
freeze-drying to preserve the body for display. The record also names concrete
biographical-looking details, including age, height, grooming, stomach contents,
and injuries. RUINS treats those as claim surfaces, not a complete biography.

## What RUINS Refuses

| Overclaim | RUINS response |
|---|---|

| Grooming and armband prove social rank. | Social role needs separate comparative custody. |
| Stomach contents explain motive. | Diet and death motive are separate. |
| Lindow II, III, and IV can be collapsed into one body. | Related remains require object-level custody. |
| Display settles the ethics of human remains. | Display is an afterlife layer; ethics belongs to a sibling system. |

## Method Result

Lindow Man promotes preserved body taphonomy custody to Core. Tollund showed how
a vivid face, noose, last meal, and conserved head can become a total story.
Lindow shows how violent injury, body-part cataloguing, conservation treatment,
and display can become motive and social-role certainty. The reusable rule is:
preserved human remains need taphonomy, recovery, forensics, conservation,
sampling, display, and public-memory gates before any biography-like claim.
