# London Mithraeum and the Reconstructed Window

London Mithraeum is an overbuilt urban ruin whose public evidence passes through
redevelopment, relocation, reconstruction, listing, and immersive display. That
makes it a second test for urban overbuild window custody after Templo Mayor.

The current London Mithraeum site says Bloomberg SPACE returns the Roman Temple
of Mithras to the location of its discovery and displays the ancient temple,
Roman artefacts from recent excavation, and contemporary art. The official
Temple of Mithras page describes reconstructed remains of the temple discovered
in 1954 during excavations after the Blitz, with haze, light, and sound used to
evoke Roman London. Those are valuable access layers, but they are not direct
unmediated access to the Roman building.

Historic England records the earlier listed reconstruction on Queen Victoria
Street. It identifies the Mithraeum as a mid-third-century Roman temple, notes
that the reconstruction stood around 6 m above and 75 m northwest of the
original site, that its orientation differed from the original, and that some
foundations and lower courses were reconstructed with modern mortar. MOLA adds
that this earlier reconstruction was about 100 m from the original findspot and
was not entirely sympathetic to the original remains.

The current display therefore cannot be read as a simple original. The evidence
layers are: original Roman fabric, modern reconstruction materials, relocation
history, original discovery context, redevelopment context, recent excavation
objects, visitor atmosphere, listing history, and public memory. Each supports a
different claim grade.

The safe publication claim is narrow: London Mithraeum is a strong case for how
an overbuilt urban ruin can survive through relocation and reconstruction while
remaining publicly meaningful. It is not a complete original context, a solved
Mithraic ritual program, a complete Roman London plan, or a display where sound,
haze, and lights become evidence of ancient practice.
