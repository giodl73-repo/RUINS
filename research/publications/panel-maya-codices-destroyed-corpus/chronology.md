# Chronology: Maya Codices Destroyed Corpus

**Owner:** CALENDAR
**Status:** working chronology

| Layer | Event/process | RUINS significance |
|---|---|---|
| Pre-Hispanic period | Maya screenfold books are produced and used. | Original book culture exceeds the surviving witness set. |
| ca. 1021-1154 | INAH reports radiocarbon range for the Codice Maya de Mexico. | Authentication and dating are custody layers. |
| Later Postclassic | Dresden, Madrid, and Paris are generally treated as late pre-Hispanic / early contact-era witnesses. | Surviving objects have distinct dates and contexts. |
| 1562 | Destruction of Maya writings under Diego de Landa is part of the source-scoped loss history. | Destruction event is not a full corpus inventory. |
| 18th-19th centuries | Dresden, Madrid, and Paris enter European scholarly/library custody. | Rediscovery and classification reshape evidence access. |
| 1971 | The Grolier/Mexico codex is exhibited publicly and becomes controversial. | Authenticity debate becomes part of custody. |
| 2015-2018 | Scholarly and INAH authentication work reframes the Mexico codex. | Scientific authentication changes claim grade. |
| Present | Digital images, facsimiles, exhibitions, and public memory mediate access. | Reproduction is a separate evidence layer. |

## Chronology Rule

Do not collapse production, destruction, colonial transfer, rediscovery,
facsimile reproduction, authentication, and modern memory into one survival
story.
