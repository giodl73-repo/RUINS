# Maya Codices Destroyed Corpus

**Authors:** draft
**Status:** stable working paper
**Date:** 2026-07-05

## Abstract

The surviving pre-Hispanic Maya codices are among the most intense RUINS cases:
a handful of material witnesses stands beside a much larger destroyed and
unrecoverable book culture. This paper treats the codices as a destroyed-corpus
problem. It does not ask what the Maya "books" were in general from four
survivors. It asks how responsible reconstruction changes when survival,
destruction, rediscovery, facsimile mediation, and authenticity debate are all
part of the evidence bridge.

## 1. Why This Is A RUINS Case

The Maya codices test absence at corpus scale. The surviving objects are direct
material evidence, but the corpus they imply is mostly absent. The surviving
set is not just small; it is filtered by colonial violence, physical fragility,
European and Mexican collecting histories, facsimile transmission, and modern
authentication work.

## 3. What Is Missing Or Damaged

| Missing bridge | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Original corpus size and range | The surviving set cannot define the whole lost book tradition. |
| Provenance for some objects | Later custody can obscure how a codex left its original context. |
| Direct destruction inventory | Book-burning reports do not enumerate the destroyed corpus. |
| Stable facsimile fidelity | Reproductions can preserve, redraw, regularize, or repair damaged signs. |
| Translation custody | A glyph image, facsimile, transcription, and translation are different evidence layers. |

## 4. Claim Grades

| Claim | Grade | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| A very small set of pre-Hispanic Maya manuscripts survives. | Source-locked | Institutional sources identify four extant witnesses. |
| The Dresden, Madrid, Paris, and Mexico codices have different custody and damage histories. | Corroborated | Current source lock separates the objects and their custody layers. |
| The surviving codices represent the full range of Maya book culture. | Refuse | Survival is too filtered and uneven. |
| Diego de Landa destroyed all Maya books. | Refuse | One reported destruction event is not a total corpus ledger. |
| The Mexico codex is usable as a surviving Maya codex. | Source-specific | Allowed through INAH/Getty/Coe-Houston-Miller-Taube custody, with authenticity history named. |

| Reconstruction | Status | RUINS treatment |

| Facsimile sequences | Mediated reconstruction | Label edition and reproduction layer. |

## 6. Cultural Memory

| Memory form | What it does | RUINS handling |
|---|---|---|
| "Only four books survived" | Makes loss legible through a sharp number. | Useful but must stay object-scoped. |
| Burning of books | Turns corpus loss into a vivid moral image. | Preserve as destruction memory, not total ledger. |
| Oldest book of the Americas | Elevates the Mexico codex after authenticity debate. | Source-scoped public/scholarly afterlife. |
| Lost Maya science | Converts calendars and astronomy into civilizational-loss shorthand. | Classify as afterlife unless claim-specific. |

## 7. Refusals

RUINS refuses the following claims in this stable working paper:

- It will not infer total Maya book culture from four surviving witnesses.
- It will not quote or paraphrase Landa as primary proof without direct edition
  custody.
- It will not claim a complete destruction count.
- It will not treat facsimiles, photos, or digital pages as the objects
  themselves.
- It will not publish codex translation claims without specialist textual
  custody.
- It will not flatten the Mexico codex into either "fake" or "settled" without
  naming the authentication source layer.

## 9. Conclusion

The Maya codices are not a window that simply restores a lost library. They are
survivors of a broken corpus. RUINS should let them remain singular, material,
and precious without making them carry the whole absent book world.
