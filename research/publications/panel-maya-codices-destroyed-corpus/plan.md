# Plan: Maya Codices Destroyed Corpus

**Module:** RUINS
**Track:** Evidence and Absence
**Status:** publication-ready
**Position in chain:** post-Herculaneum case testing deliberate textual destruction

## Research Question

How should RUINS describe a book tradition when only a tiny set of pre-Hispanic
Maya screenfold manuscripts survives, while the broader corpus is known through
destruction reports, later copies, facsimiles, inscriptions, and cultural memory?

## Core Argument

The Maya codices are a RUINS case because the surviving witnesses are both
extraordinary and dangerously unrepresentative. Dresden, Madrid, Paris, and the
Codice Maya de Mexico give direct material access to pre-Hispanic Maya book
practice, but they cannot stand in for the destroyed corpus as a whole. RUINS
must separate surviving books, reported book-burning, colonial and modern
custody, facsimile history, authenticity debate, and the larger absence of Maya
book culture.

## Required Boundaries

1. Do not infer the full range of Maya books from the four extant codices.
2. Do not turn Diego de Landa into the sole cause of all corpus loss.
3. Do not quote destruction passages or translated codex text without direct
   source custody.
4. Do not treat facsimile editions as the objects themselves.
5. Do not treat the Mexico codex authenticity history as settled by public
   shorthand alone; cite the specific custody layer used.
