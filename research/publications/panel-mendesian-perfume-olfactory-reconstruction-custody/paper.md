# Mendesian Perfume and Olfactory Reconstruction Custody

Mendesian perfume is a RUINS case where the missing object is smell. The modern
reconstruction combines archaeology, texts, paleobotany, residue work, and
experimental making, but the result is not the ancient scent itself. RUINS
therefore grades the chain between material traces and sensory afterlife.

The University of Hawaii account anchors the archaeological setting. The Tell
Timai Project excavated ancient Thmuis in the Nile Delta. The page identifies
Thmuis as home to the famous Mendesian and Metopian perfumes, and says the
project uncovered evidence of the ancient fragrance industry. That evidence
includes third-century BCE kilns, fine perfume bottles, a possible later glass
kiln for small unguentaria, a liquid-production area, and ongoing residue
analysis of amphora contents. The same page says researchers recreated the
perfume based on formulas in ancient Greek texts.

The scholarly record sharpens the method. The Nottingham Trent repository
abstract for "Eau de Cleopatra: Mendesian Perfume and Tell Timai" says Classics,
Egyptology, and experimental archaeology were used to recreate the perfume.
Classical sources and paleobotany were especially important for identifying
Egyptian sacred oils such as camphor and balanos, and excavations at Tell Timai
revealed a perfumery that contributed to reconstructing the manufacturing
process. The Tell Timai publication page repeats that this ancient Mendesian
perfume was recreated in the lab, exhibited at the Smithsonian, and worn again.

That evidence supports a strong olfactory-reconstruction custody claim. It does
not support an exact ancient-smell claim. A modern batch may follow a defensible
recipe hypothesis, but ancient ingredients, plant identifications, oil bases,
processing time, storage, oxidation, climate, vessel residue, batch variation,
wearing context, and human perception remain separate layers.

The Cleopatra layer needs special discipline. "Cleopatra's perfume" is powerful
public shorthand, but current evidence supports a Mendesian reconstruction from
a region and recipe tradition, not direct access to Cleopatra's body, room,
wardrobe, or personal bottle. Exhibition scent is a valuable access layer, not
the ancient atmosphere itself.
