# The Met Sphinx and Lost Polychromy Trace Custody

The Met sphinx is a RUINS case where the stone survives but a major evidence
layer has largely vanished: the painted surface. Public knowledge is mediated by
surviving pigment traces, scientific imaging, samples, chemical identifications,
physical reconstruction, and digital experience.

The Met's Chroma exhibition states that ancient Greek and Roman sculpture was
once colorful and richly adorned, and that new discoveries of surviving ancient
color are being identified through scientific methods. Its overview makes the
reconstruction boundary explicit: colored reconstructions are presented beside
original works and result from analytical techniques, 3D imaging, and art
historical research.

The focused Met sphinx article gives the custody layers. The sphinx retained
extensive original pigment remains. Research used non-invasive and minimally
invasive techniques, digital microscopy, multiband imaging, X-ray fluorescence,
and microsamples to identify pigments including Egyptian blue, azurite,
carbon-based black, cinnabar, red ocher, yellow ocher, and gypsum. The
Brinkmann reconstruction incorporated the Met's scientific research, and an AR
experience let visitors examine the sphinx and the research behind the
reconstruction.

This paper promotes lost polychromy trace custody. It separates pigment trace,
visible remnant, sample, imaging output, chemical identification, historical
comparison, physical reconstruction, AR experience, and modern white-marble
memory. The safe publication claim is narrow: the Met sphinx is strong evidence
that ancient sculpture could carry elaborate color, but it is not a complete
original color scheme for every surface, final funerary meaning, complete
painterly method, object-equivalent restoration, or total Greek aesthetic.
