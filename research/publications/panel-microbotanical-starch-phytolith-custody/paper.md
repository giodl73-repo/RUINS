# Plant Microremains and Microbotanical Custody

Starch grains, phytoliths, pollen, and other plant microremains can make
otherwise invisible plant use visible. They can survive on tools, in dental
calculus, in ceramic residues, and in sediments, and they can support bounded
claims about plant presence, processing, and residue formation.

The evidence is powerful but fragile. A starch grain is not automatically an
exact species. A phytolith morphotype is not domestication proof. Dental
calculus is not a direct meal, and a tool residue is not a complete tool
function. Microbotanical interpretation depends on preservation, contamination,
extraction protocol, reference collections, taxonomic uncertainty, processing
damage, context, quantification, and whether the trace came from use, burial,
handling, or modern contamination.

RUINS promotes microbotanical starch-phytolith custody to Core here. Plant
microremains can support bounded plant-use and residue claims, but diet,
domestication, crop adoption, tool function, recipes, medicine, craft, storage,
seasonality, and complete plant-use history require separate custody.
