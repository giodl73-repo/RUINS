# Midas Touch and Gustatory Reconstruction Custody

The beverage associated with the Gordion Tumulus MM funerary banquet is a RUINS
case where a lost taste is reconstructed from residue and made public through a
modern drink. It is not simply "the ancient beer came back." The evidence chain
runs through residue, marker compounds, ingredient classes, banquet context,
recipe inference, brewing choices, product identity, and legend.

Penn Museum's funerary banquet account anchors the archaeological setting and
the rarity of preserved organic materials. Related museum and public sources
describe residues from drinking vessels associated with the tomb and identify a
mixed fermented beverage involving grape wine, barley beer, and honey mead.
Dogfish Head's Midas Touch page presents the modern drink as made with
ingredients found in 2,700-year-old drinking vessels from the tomb and describes
it as somewhere between beer, wine, and mead.

That evidence supports a strong gustatory-reconstruction claim: the modern
drink is a research-inspired reconstruction from residue-detected ingredient
classes. It does not support exact ancient taste. Residues do not preserve
proportions, yeast ecology, water chemistry, fermentation temperature, vessel
aging, serving conditions, mouthfeel, batch variation, or the sensory world of a
funerary banquet. Commercial brewing also adds modern product constraints.

The Midas layer needs discipline. The tomb is entangled with Midas and Gordias
memory, and the modern product invites "drink like Midas" framing. RUINS keeps
that as public afterlife, not proof that Midas personally consumed the drink or
that a modern bottle recreates an original meal.
