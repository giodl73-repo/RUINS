# Mostar Bridge and the Reconstructed Void

Mostar's Old Bridge is not simply a recovered monument. It is a destroyed bridge
whose absence, reconstruction, exposed remnants, restored surroundings, and
symbolic afterlife must remain separately graded.

UNESCO states that most of historic Mostar and the Old Bridge were destroyed in
the 1990s conflict, that the bridge was rebuilt in 2004, and that many Old Town
edifices were restored or rebuilt with the contribution of a UNESCO-established
international scientific committee. The reconstructed bridge and Old City are
framed as symbols of reconciliation, international cooperation, and coexistence.

UNESCO's Outstanding Universal Value statement makes the reconstruction custody
more precise. The property regained testimonial value after reconstruction, but
the reconstruction was based on documentation, authentic materials and
techniques, and exposed remaining original material in a museum. UNESCO also
states that the reconstruction should be understood as background to the
restoration of intangible dimensions, not as a simple erasure of destruction.

The UNESCO Mostar story adds the public-memory layer: the bridge was completely
destroyed on 9 November 1993, the ruined bridge came to symbolize the destruction
of heritage, a debate followed over memorial preservation versus reconstruction,
and the reconstruction used the same local quarry while remnants and older
medieval structures were displayed to preserve multilayered memory.

This paper promotes monumental void reconstruction custody to Core. Mostar adds
a reconstructed-span version of the Bamiyan problem: the void did not remain
empty, but rebuilding still cannot become original fabric, total recovery,
community consensus, final symbolic meaning, or a complete account of the city.
The safe publication claim is narrow: Mostar is a strong case for source-locked
reconstruction after deliberate destruction, with the rebuilt bridge acting as
both material reconstruction and memory layer.
