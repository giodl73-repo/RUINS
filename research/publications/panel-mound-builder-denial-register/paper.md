# Mound Builder Denial Register

**Module:** RUINS
**Status:** publication-ready
**Date:** 2026-07-05

## Abstract

North American mounds and earthworks test RUINS because their surviving
evidence was repeatedly overwritten by a denial story. The "Mound Builder"
myth imagined vanished races, giants, ancient visitors, or other non-Native
builders in order to avoid crediting Indigenous peoples with monumental
architecture. RUINS treats that mythology as interpretive violence and cultural
memory, not as archaeology. The responsible reconstruction problem is to keep
earthwork evidence, current Indigenous-authorship claims, historic denial,
public mystery framing, and living heritage in separate grades.

## Why This Tests The Capability

Great Zimbabwe tested colonial denial in a southern African drystone city.
Mound Builder denial tests the same capability in North American earthworks,
where the overclaim pressure comes from settler archaeology, racial hierarchy,
removal-era politics, antiquarian speculation, and later pseudoarchaeology.

The danger is the same: a complex local achievement becomes an ownerless
mystery. RUINS refuses that compression.

## Evidence Layers

| Surface | Evidence condition | Current grade |
|---|---|---|
| Mounds and earthworks | direct material / landscape evidence | site-specific Indigenous monumental evidence |
| Excavation and archive reports | mediated archaeological record | source-specific custody, not complete identity map |
| Artifacts and raw materials | material and exchange evidence | contact/exchange, not outsider authorship |
| "Lost race" narratives | denial / cultural-memory evidence | interpretive violence, not live archaeology |
| Smithsonian / Bureau of Ethnology history | corrective archive layer | historical debunking and professional archaeology surface |
| UNESCO / NPS / Ohio heritage pages | public heritage and education | high-level current public custody |
| Indigenous sacred memory | living memory / heritage custody | sensitive afterlife and relation layer |

## Claim Grades

| Claim | Grade | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| Hopewell earthworks were built by American Indian / Indigenous communities. | Corroborated public heritage claim | NPS, UNESCO, and Hopewell heritage sources support high-level authorship. |
| The "lost race" Mound Builder theory denied Native capability. | Corroborated denial-history claim | Penn Museum and related scholarship support this as interpretive history. |
| Cyrus Thomas / Bureau work is an important corrective archive layer. | Source-specific | Smithsonian metadata locks the report; detailed conclusions need passage custody. |
| Earthwork geometry proves ancient visitors, giants, or non-Native builders. | Refuse | Complexity is not outsider-authorship evidence. |
| One named culture, tribe, or modern community built every mound. | Refuse | Mound building spans many places, periods, communities, and site types. |
| Heritage recognition proves every site interpretation. | Refuse | UNESCO/NPS support public custody and high-level values, not every detailed claim. |

## Register Pattern

The reusable colonial-denial register asks:

1. What survives materially?
2. What current source-scoped archaeology says about authorship?
3. What denial narrative displaced local or Indigenous agency?
4. Which public words revive denial by making the site mysterious, ownerless,
   vanished, or impossible?
5. What living-memory or heritage layer must be respected without turning it
   into total proof of every original-state claim?

## Refusals

RUINS refuses the following claims in this paper:

- a vanished non-Native race built the mounds;
- giants, ancient astronauts, Israelites, Vikings, Welsh, Toltecs, or other
  visitors explain Indigenous earthworks;
- earthwork scale, geometry, or astronomical alignment proves outsider origin;
- trade or distant raw materials prove foreign authorship;
- all mounds belong to one people, one date, one function, or one story;
- "mysterious" framing where the mystery depends on denying Indigenous skill;
- excavation, collecting, or site-disturbance guidance.

## Conclusion

Mound Builder denial makes the colonial-denial register portable. The method is
not only to correct the false attribution. It is to preserve the damaged
interpretive layer as evidence of how ruins can be made ownerless by narrative.
That makes Great Zimbabwe and Mound Builder denial a matched pair: two
different monumental traditions, one reusable refusal discipline.
