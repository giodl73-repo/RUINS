# Nasca and the Custody of Scale-Mediated Visibility

Nasca is not only a set of marks on desert ground. It is a case where the scale
at which the evidence becomes legible can change the claim being made.

UNESCO describes the Lines and Geoglyphs of Nasca and Palpa as a vast arid
surface landscape south of Lima, where geoglyphs and lines were scratched into
the ground over many centuries. The Peruvian Ministry of Culture frames them as
zoomorphic, anthropomorphic, line, and swept-field figures of great magnitude,
variety, and geometric precision on an extensive desert surface. Both framings
support a strong RUINS baseline: the surviving evidence is a modified ground
surface, not a detached object and not a simple image.

That baseline matters because public knowledge of Nasca often arrives from
above. Aerial photographs, satellite imagery, radar images, maps, and public
outlines can make figures readable at a glance. They can also quietly transform
surface traces into a picture puzzle. A modern aerial view is evidence of modern
visibility. It is not automatically evidence that ancient builders intended an
aerial viewer, that all figures are equally visible from above, or that all
figures share one function.

Recent remote-sensing work makes the custody problem sharper. The Peruvian
Ministry of Culture has described aerial and satellite radar imagery as a tool
for identifying fragile terrain and human disturbance. Yamagata University's
2024 release on the PNAS study reports that AI helped identify hundreds of new
figurative geoglyphs for field survey and distinguishes giant line-type figures
from smaller relief-type figures associated with trails. These are powerful
knowledge tools, but they are not interchangeable. A radar image can support
monitoring. An AI candidate can prioritize fieldwork. Field confirmation can
turn a candidate into a documented geoglyph. None of those steps, by itself,
settles ancient function.

Nasca therefore creates a new RUINS active capability: scale-mediated surface
visibility custody. The paper must separate ground trace, path-scale viewing,
aerial visibility, satellite/radar detection, AI candidate status, field
confirmation, map outline, damage map, dating, and ritual or astronomical
interpretation. If those layers collapse, the site becomes a mystery image
rather than a controlled survival problem.

The responsible claim is narrow. Nasca can demonstrate how a surface survival
may become more legible through modern viewing systems without those systems
becoming ancient intention, complete inventory, final function, or whole-culture
explanation. RUINS owns that custody problem; geography, remote-sensing
engineering, ritual interpretation, and broader Nasca history belong to sibling
systems.
