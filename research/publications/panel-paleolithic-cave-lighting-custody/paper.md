# Paleolithic Cave Lighting and Reconstructed Visual Conditions

The 2021 PLOS ONE cave-lighting experiment is a useful RUINS pressure case
because it reconstructs a sensory condition without recovering an ancient
experience. The study characterizes wooden torches, portable fat lamps, and
fireplaces through empirical observations and experimental archaeology in a
cave context.

RUINS treats the result as strong evidence for a method boundary: lighting
systems can be compared for duration, smoke, light behavior, mobility, and
scene suitability, but those measurements do not become direct access to how
Paleolithic people saw, intended, or ritually understood cave art.

The paper also reports application of estimated luminous data to Atxurra cave
through 3D/GIS modelling. That creates a second layer: experiment-derived
values are transported into a modelled cave-art context. The transfer is useful,
but it must remain labelled as reconstructed visibility, not original visual
experience.

The RUINS rule is simple: reconstructed firelight may illuminate a hypothesis,
but it cannot silently become ancient perception.
