# Palmyra Replica Reconstruction

**Authors:** draft
**Status:** stable working paper
**Date:** 2026-07-05

## Abstract

Palmyra tests a modern ruin condition: a damaged World Heritage site is followed
by satellite confirmation, emergency conservation language, public replicas,
digital modelling, and formal reconstruction governance. This paper argues that
RUINS must grade these layers separately. A replica can be evidence of
documentation, public memory, technical capacity, and resistance to cultural
erasure. It is not the original monument, not a substitute for field assessment,
and not proof that the site has been restored.

## 1. Why This Is A RUINS Case

Palmyra is not only a destroyed monument story. It is a case where later
reconstruction signals are powerful enough to overtake the damaged site:

- UNESCO records Palmyra's Outstanding Universal Value and World Heritage in
  Danger status.
- ISIS/Daesh destruction damaged named monuments and became global media.
- The Institute for Digital Archaeology produced a public scale reproduction of
  the Triumphal Arch.
- UNESCO recovery governance distinguishes emergency measures, documentation,
  restoration methodology, and reconstruction decisions.
- Public memory often compresses all this into "Palmyra rebuilt."

## 3. What Is Missing Or Damaged

| Missing bridge | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Full direct field assessment for every damaged attribute | Reconstruction claims need more than public images. |
| Original material continuity for destroyed features | Replica material is not original fabric. |
| Stable public distinction between replica and restoration | Public display can imply recovery. |
| Final restoration governance for every feature | UNESCO decisions require methodology and review. |
| Complete model provenance for public replicas | Scale, source images, machining, material, and authority must be named. |

## 5. Replica Status

| Object/model | RUINS status | Boundary |
|---|---|---|
| Damaged original Arch of Triumph | Original site feature / damaged original | Owned by site custody and conservation governance. |
| IDA/Oxford arch | Replica / public facsimile | Evidence of documentation and memory, not original fabric. |
| Digital model | Mediated documentation/model | Useful only with dataset, method, scale, and uncertainty. |
| Future site restoration | Conservation/reconstruction intervention | Requires governance and field assessment custody. |

## 6. Cultural Memory

| Memory form | What it does | RUINS handling |
|---|---|---|
| "You cannot erase history" | Frames replication as resistance. | Afterlife evidence. |
| Replica touring capitals | Makes Palmyra visible to publics far from Syria. | Public-memory surface. |
| Digital salvation narrative | Makes technical reproduction feel like recovery. | Downgrade unless status is named. |
| World Heritage recovery discourse | Converts site damage into governance and international duty. | Governance record, not automatic restoration. |

## 7. Refusals

RUINS refuses the following claims in this paper:

- It will not treat a replica as the original.
- It will not treat a digital model as a restoration plan.
- It will not treat public display as site recovery.
- It will not claim reconstruction approval without UNESCO/state-party custody.
- It will not treat Palmyra as only a Western heritage-loss symbol.
- It will not provide conservation, engineering, or site-intervention advice.

## 9. Conclusion

Palmyra shows why RUINS needs replica-status custody. A replica may be valuable,
moving, and technically impressive. It may also be a new object with its own
politics and memory. Responsible reconstruction begins by refusing to let that
new object silently stand in for the damaged original.
