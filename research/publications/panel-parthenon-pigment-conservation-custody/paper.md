# Parthenon Pigment Conservation Custody

The Parthenon sculptures are a RUINS case where stone survives but color is
partial, fragile, and method-mediated. The evidence condition is not "the
Parthenon was colored" as a total claim. It is a layered custody problem:
visible pigment, VIL detection, XRF analysis, cleaning, surface condition,
display access, and modern white-marble memory.

The British Museum describes a 2018 conservation project on Parthenon sculptures
using non-invasive visible-induced infrared luminescence and X-ray fluorescence.
Cleaning removed dust and ingrained dirt through individually evaluated methods,
and that process revealed original pigments. The Museum also states that
architectural elements and sculptures were originally painted, while still
showing that the surviving evidence is partial: some Egyptian blue was visible
to the naked eye, larger areas were visible only under certain light conditions,
and VIL could detect small remnants.

This makes Parthenon pigment conservation a second test for lost polychromy
trace custody. Compared with the Met sphinx, where reconstruction and AR are
central, Parthenon evidence is tied to conservation work, surface access, and
method-dependent detection. Both cases require the same refusal discipline:
trace does not become complete color; method output does not become final
appearance; pigment name does not become painterly method; and public
white-marble memory does not define ancient aesthetics.

The safe publication claim is narrow: Parthenon sculptures preserve important
traces of lost color, especially Egyptian blue, and scientific conservation has
made some traces newly visible or detectable. That does not settle complete
original color distribution, divine or ritual meaning, full finishing sequence,
or the total aesthetics of Greek sculpture.
