# Parthenon Sculptures Dispersed Custody

**Module:** RUINS
**Status:** publication-ready
**Date:** 2026-07-05

## Abstract

The Parthenon Sculptures test dispersed-object custody because the objects
survive, but no single custody surface answers the case. The British Museum
holds a major group removed by Lord Elgin under an Ottoman-era permit claim and
entered into the Museum by Act of Parliament. The Acropolis Museum displays
Greek-held originals in a gallery oriented to the Parthenon's cella, while using
plaster copies for sculptures retained elsewhere. RUINS treats these as separate
layers: original monument, surviving fragment, removal, legal acquisition,
current holding, display strategy, copy, request, loan, and public memory.

## Why This Tests The Capability

Benin Bronzes tested violent looting, global dispersal, digital reconnection,
and partial return. Parthenon Sculptures test a different dispersed-object
surface: contested removal authority, museum title, formal return request, split
display, and replica/recontextual display.

The shared danger is closure by one layer. Current possession is not original
context. Legal title is not ethical or interpretive closure. Gallery alignment
is not object reunification. A plaster copy is not the fragment.

## Claim Grades

| Claim | Grade | Rule |
|---|---|---|

| Elgin removed about half of the remaining sculptures between 1801 and 1805 under a permit claim. | Source-locked to British Museum wording | Do not convert to legal adjudication. |
| A formal Greek request for permanent return was first made in 1983. | Source-locked | British Museum supports the request layer. |
| The Acropolis Museum combines original marble sculptures with plaster copies of those retained elsewhere. | Source-locked | Acropolis Museum supports display/recontextualization claims. |
| The Parthenon can be completely reconstructed by moving one collection. | Refuse | British Museum notes much original decoration is lost; display/reunification is not total reconstruction. |
| Legal title, ethical claim, and display context are the same thing. | Refuse | These are separate custody layers. |
| A plaster copy or digital record is object-equivalent. | Refuse | It is a mediated access/display layer. |

## Dispersed-Custody Ladder

1. Identify object group and fragment category.
2. Record original monument context only where source-scoped.
3. Separate removal story, legal/governance claim, and current holding.
4. Separate return request, loan condition, and settlement from possession.
5. Treat gallery alignment, replicas, and copies as display strategies.
6. Treat world-museum and reunification narratives as public-memory layers.
7. Refuse total reconstruction, total title closure, and copy-object equivalence.

## Conclusion

The Parthenon Sculptures make dispersed-object custody Core. They show that the
method works beyond looted palace objects and restitution ceremonies. RUINS can
track a surviving object group through broken context, removal, institutional
holding, display, copy, and return memory without pretending to decide law,
diplomacy, or final ownership.
