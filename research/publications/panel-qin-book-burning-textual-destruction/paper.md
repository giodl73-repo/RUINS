# Qin Book Burning Textual Destruction

**Module:** RUINS
**Status:** publication-ready
**Date:** 2026-07-05

## Abstract

The Qin "burning of books" is a RUINS case because deliberate textual
destruction is visible, but its effects are not self-inventorying. A source
tradition mediated through Sima Qian preserves Li Si's proposal to suppress
politically dangerous private texts and non-Qin histories. Modern summaries and
teaching editions also stress exemptions, Han-period mediation, and later
critique of the joined "burning books and burying scholars" image. RUINS treats
the event as deliberate textual destruction with bounded custody: a destruction
order can be source-locked without becoming a complete catalogue of loss.

## Why This Tests The Capability

Maya codices tested a surviving-witness problem: a tiny extant set and a
destruction report could be mistaken for the whole absent corpus. Qin book
burning tests a different surface: an explicit policy report can be mistaken
for complete event certainty, total intellectual rupture, and destroyed
inventory.

The capability is distinct from general destroyed-corpus custody because the
central risk is policy-image compression. A deliberate act can be real and still
not answer all corpus, inventory, quotation, transmission, or memory questions.

## Destruction-Custody Ladder

1. Identify the destruction report and its source mediation.
2. Name target categories before naming losses.
3. Record exemptions and official/technical survival layers.
4. Separate related violence against scholars from book destruction.
5. Treat later tyranny/censorship memory as afterlife evidence.
6. Refuse destroyed inventories, total intellectual rupture, and exact
   quotation until direct source custody is added.

## Refusals

RUINS refuses the following claims in this paper:

- a complete inventory of books burned by Qin;
- exact destroyed title counts or genre maps;
- total destruction of the Confucian classics or all pre-Qin thought;
- treating the scholar-execution story as automatically settled because book
  burning is source-supported;
- exact Chinese quotation, English translation, or philological interpretation;
- one imperial policy as the entire loss history of Chinese texts;
- later moral memory as proof of event details.

## Conclusion

Qin book burning promotes deliberate textual destruction to Core because it
shows the method outside the destroyed-witness problem of Maya codices. The
proper RUINS move is not to weaken the deliberate-destruction claim. It is to
prevent that claim from doing too much work. A destruction order is evidence of
intentional textual suppression; it is not by itself a catalogue, corpus map,
translation lock, or civilizational endpoint.
