# Plan: Qin Book Burning Textual Destruction

**Module:** RUINS
**Track:** Evidence and Absence
**Status:** publication-ready
**Position in chain:** second deliberate-textual-destruction case after Maya codices

## Research Question

How should RUINS handle the Qin "burning of books" when the source tradition
preserves an explicit destruction policy, but later narration, exemptions,
survivals, archive loss, and the scholar-execution image complicate total-loss
claims?

## Core Argument

Qin book burning is deliberate textual destruction, but not a complete destroyed
inventory. The current RUINS-safe claim is that a Sima Qian-mediated Li Si
memorial tradition reports a 213 BCE policy aimed at politically dangerous
private texts and rival histories, with exemptions and official/technical
survival layers. RUINS refuses to turn that report into a full map of what was
lost, a complete explanation of Chinese textual transmission, or a settled
literal image of all later "burning books and burying scholars" memory.

## Required Boundaries

1. Keep Li Si, Sima Qian, Han-period mediation, and modern translation layers
   visible.
2. Separate book-burning policy from the scholar-execution tradition.
3. Record exemptions and survivals before making loss claims.
4. Do not infer destroyed titles, counts, genres, or total intellectual rupture.
5. Hand exact Chinese text, translation, early Chinese philosophy, and Qin/Han
   political history to sibling systems.

## Promotion Decision

This paper, paired with Maya codices, promotes
`ruins-capability:deliberate-textual-destruction` to Core. The reusable method
is the destruction-custody gate: source tradition, target category, exemptions,
survival, corpus effect, and memory image must be kept separate.
