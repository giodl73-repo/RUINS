# Radiocarbon Reservoir Effects and Old-Wood Custody

Radiocarbon dating is one of archaeology's core chronological tools, but it is
not a direct event recorder. A measured radiocarbon age must pass through
sample identification, pretreatment, calibration, reservoir assessment, context,
and modelling before it can support archaeological timing.

Reservoir effects are especially strong RUINS triggers. Marine and freshwater
carbon can make samples appear older than associated terrestrial material, and
freshwater hardwater effects can vary locally. Old wood creates a different
offset: charcoal or timber may date the growth of carbon in a long-lived tree,
not the event under study. Bayesian models, outlier tools, summed probability
distributions, and wiggle matches help structure uncertainty, but they do not
make model output equal to historical fact.

RUINS promotes radiocarbon reservoir old-wood custody to Core here. Radiocarbon
results can support bounded chronological pressure, but exact event dates,
occupation dates, burial dates, transition boundaries, population curves,
migration proof, collapse timing, diet proof, pottery-use dates, and solved
chronologies require separate custody.
