# Chronology: Roman Concrete Partial Survival

**Owner:** Absence Historian
**Status:** working chronology

## Layers

| Layer | Approximate horizon | Evidence condition | RUINS note |
|---|---|---|---|
| Roman material practice | Republican / Imperial periods | Surviving structures, sampled mortars, textual descriptions | Not one uniform formula. |
| Ancient technical writing | Vitruvius and other textual witnesses | copied text / translation | Describes materials and practice, but not full field variability. |
| Survival of structures | Antiquity to present | direct material survival under changing environments | Survival is evidence of performance in specific conditions. |
| Loss or transformation of practice | post-antique / modern cement history | historical process | A technique may be partially forgotten, replaced, or transformed rather than erased. |
| Scientific characterization | 20th-21st century | lab analysis, microscopy, mineralogy, isotopes | Creates modern evidence surfaces not available to ancient builders. |
| Modern reconstruction | 21st century | experimental / engineering translation | Inspired by ancient evidence; not identical to ancient practice. |
| Public memory | contemporary media | cultural-memory evidence | "Secret Roman concrete" turns a bounded materials question into a mythic lost-tech story. |

## Chronology Controls

1. Do not present Vitruvius as a complete recipe book for all Roman concrete.
2. Do not present marine concrete findings as universal wall-concrete findings.
3. Do not treat modern lab reconstruction as recovered ancient practice unless
   the source explicitly supports that narrower claim.
4. Do not treat long survival as proof that all Roman concrete was superior to
   all modern concrete.
