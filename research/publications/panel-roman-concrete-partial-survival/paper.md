# Roman Concrete Partial Survival

**Authors:** draft
**Status:** publication-ready
**Date:** 2026-07-04

## Abstract

Roman concrete is a RUINS case because the technique is not simply lost. Texts
survive. Structures survive. Mortar samples survive. Modern labs can identify
mineral phases, lime clasts, and reaction pathways. Yet the bridge between
ancient practice and modern reconstruction is broken in several places. This
paper treats Roman concrete as a partially surviving technical tradition whose
evidence must be separated into textual recipe, material sample, environmental
performance, laboratory model, and modern lost-secret memory.

## 1. Why This Is A RUINS Case

Roman concrete is often framed as a mystery solved by one missing ingredient or
one rediscovered trick. That framing is too simple.

The technique survives through:

- ancient writing,
- buildings and maritime structures,
- sampled mortars and aggregates,
- mineral and microstructural analysis,
- modern experimental reconstruction,
- popular stories about Roman superiority and lost knowledge.

The absence is not total disappearance. It is the loss of a continuous,
fully-documented practice chain connecting ancient material selection, labor,
site workflow, environmental setting, and long-term performance.

## 2. What Survives

| Surviving trace | Evidence condition | Claim grade |
|---|---|---|
| Ancient recipe and material descriptions | copied textual witness / translation-dependent | source-dependent |
| Standing structures and maritime remains | direct material survival | observed |
| Mortar, aggregate, and lime-clast samples | direct material through sampling | source-specific |
| Mineral phases such as Al-tobermorite / phillipsite | lab characterization | source-specific |
| Hot-mixing and self-healing models | laboratory inference and experiment | model-dependent |
| Pompeii in-progress construction evidence | archaeological context | source-specific |
| Modern Roman-inspired concretes | experimental translation | modern artifact |
| "secret Roman concrete" stories | cultural memory | afterlife evidence |

## 3. What Is Missing

| Missing or weak bridge | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Complete ancient production record | Texts do not capture every workshop, region, material source, or structure type. |
| Universal recipe | Roman concrete varied by place, use, aggregate, pozzolan, water, and building context. |
| Continuous craft transmission | Modern researchers reconstruct from texts and material traces, not from uninterrupted practice. |
| Direct ancient explanation of durability mechanisms | Ancient builders did not describe Al-tobermorite, lime-clast chemistry, or isotope signatures. |
| One-to-one modern replacement | Modern codes, reinforcement, loads, labor, sustainability constraints, and materials differ. |

## 4. Claim Grades

| Claim | Grade | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Roman concrete is partially documented and materially preserved. | Corroborated | Texts, structures, and sampled material all survive. |
| Roman concrete had one secret ingredient. | Refuse | Current evidence involves multiple materials, methods, and environments. |
| Vitruvius provides useful recipe evidence. | Corroborated with limits | Textual witness, not complete practice record. |
| Some marine Roman concretes show seawater-driven mineral evolution. | Plausible / source-specific | Source-locked with marine limit. |
| Some Roman mortars may preserve evidence of hot mixing and lime-clast self-healing. | Plausible / source-specific | Source-locked with sample/context limit. |
| Pompeii proves every Roman concrete was hot-mixed. | Refuse | One site cannot universalize empire-wide practice. |
| Modern concrete should simply copy Roman concrete. | Refuse | Modern engineering requirements are different. |

## 5. Reconstruction Status

| Reconstruction type | Status | RUINS treatment |
|---|---|---|
| Textual recipe reconstruction | Translation-mediated | Use with source and passage labels. |
| Material composition reconstruction | Laboratory characterization | Treat as sample-specific. |
| Durability mechanism | Scientific model | Useful but bounded by environment and source. |
| Ancient workflow | Archaeological inference | Stronger where in-progress sites or tools survive. |
| Modern replication | New engineering artifact | Not the same as ancient practice. |

## 6. Cultural Memory

| Memory form | What it does | RUINS handling |
|---|---|---|
| Lost Roman secret | Makes technical partial survival legible as a mystery. | Downgrade to public-memory form. |
| Roman superiority over modernity | Turns material longevity into civilizational nostalgia. | Refuse category-wide claims. |
| Green concrete inspiration | Translates ancient evidence into modern sustainability hopes. | Treat as modern engineering afterlife. |
| Miracle self-healing | Compresses bounded mechanisms into a magical property. | Require sample and mechanism labels. |

## 7. Refusals

RUINS refuses the following claims in this publication-ready paper:

- It will not say Roman concrete had one secret recipe.
- It will not treat Vitruvius as a complete field manual.
- It will not universalize marine concrete findings to all Roman concrete.
- It will not universalize Pompeii workflow evidence to the whole empire.
- It will not provide construction instructions.
- It will not claim modern engineers can simply recreate Roman concrete.

## 9. Conclusion

Roman concrete is not a clean lost-technology story. It is a partial-survival
case where ancient text, surviving structure, sampled material, and modern lab
model each preserve a different layer. RUINS should keep those layers apart so
that "lost recipe" memory does not swallow the more interesting technical
condition: a practice partly preserved by matter, partly preserved by text, and
partly reconstructed by modern science.
