# Seikilos Epitaph and Complete-Looking Performance Reconstruction

The Seikilos stele is a stronger survival case than Hurrian Hymn H6, but it
creates the same RUINS pressure. Because the song text and notation survive
together on stone, public presentation can make the lost performance seem fully
recoverable. RUINS grades that temptation rather than simply celebrating the
song's completeness.

The National Museum of Denmark identifies the stele as a Greek grave monument
from the first century CE, with an inscription for Seikilos. It says the last
part of the inscription is a hymn and that note signs appear between the lines.
The museum page describes a twelve-line inscription: five lines naming Seikilos
as dedicator and seven lines forming a short poem with musical notation in
letters and symbols. It also notes that the lower line is damaged and may refer
to Euterpe.

The same page gives the broader survival context: about fifty examples of
ancient music from classical to Roman time are known, most on papyrus, and only
three in stone. The Greek notation system was probably developed in the third
or fourth century BCE, likely used by professional composers and chorus leaders,
while others learned melodies by listening. The museum also says there is no
evidence that the Greek notation system survived into the Middle Ages.

That evidence supports a strong claim: Seikilos is a relatively complete
text-plus-notation survival case. It does not support direct ancient sound.
The stone preserves notation and words, not the original voice, tempo, delivery,
instrumental accompaniment, listening situation, social use, or funerary
performance. Modern staff transcriptions and recordings are valuable access
layers, but they are modern choices.

The public-memory risk is different from H6. H6 is vulnerable because the
notation is ambiguous and rival decipherments proliferate. Seikilos is
vulnerable because "complete" can be overread as "directly heard again." RUINS
therefore treats completeness as a high-quality evidence condition, not as
object-equivalence with the lost performance.
