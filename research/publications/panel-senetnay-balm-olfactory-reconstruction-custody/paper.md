# Senetnay Balm and Residue-Based Olfactory Reconstruction

Senetnay's "Scent of Eternity" is an unlike second test for olfactory
reconstruction custody. Mendesian perfume starts from production archaeology and
recipe tradition. Senetnay starts from residue in canopic jars that once held
mummified organs. Both produce a modern scent, and both risk being mistaken for
direct ancient smell.

Max Planck reporting says the team analyzed balm residues from two canopic jars
from the mummification equipment of Senetnay, excavated by Howard Carter from
Tomb KV42 in the Valley of the Kings and now housed in the Museum August
Kestner. The reported mixture included beeswax, plant oil, fats, bitumen,
Pinaceae resins, a balsamic substance, and dammar or Pistacia tree resin. The
Scientific Reports article describes the material as balm residue scraped from
now-empty canopic jars that once contained Senetnay's mummified organs, dating
to the 18th dynasty around 1450 BCE.

That evidence supports a strong residue-to-scent reconstruction claim. It does
not support exact ancient smell. The original balm changed through time, organ
contact, vessel storage, evaporation, oxidation, excavation, conservation,
sampling, ingredient uncertainty, and modern scent formulation. Dammar-or-
Pistacia ambiguity alone blocks any total recipe or exact scent claim.

The public display layer is valuable but risky. Max Planck and museum reporting
frame the recreated aroma as a sensory bridge to antiquity and as a museum
experience. RUINS keeps that experience as access and afterlife. The public can
smell a research-based reconstruction, not the untouched air of KV42, Senetnay's
body, or a stable 18th-dynasty odor.
