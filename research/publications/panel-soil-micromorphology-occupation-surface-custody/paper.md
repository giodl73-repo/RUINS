# Soil Micromorphology and Occupation-Surface Custody

Soil micromorphology can make thin occupation surfaces readable. Block samples
and thin sections can preserve microstratigraphy, ash, dung, plaster, trampled
sediment, plant remains, floor renewal, and other residues that excavation may
not resolve at visible scale.

The evidence is powerful but bounded. A thin section is not an exact activity
area. An ash lens is not complete hearth history. Dung spherulites are not a
penning system by themselves, and a floor lamina is not a daily-life routine.
Interpretation depends on sample context, microfacies, sediment source,
depositional pathway, post-depositional alteration, spatial sampling, analogues,
and whether absence reflects cleaning, removal, recovery, or preservation.

RUINS promotes soil micromorphology occupation-surface custody to Core here.
Micromorphological evidence can support bounded floor, hearth, dung, trample,
and activity-residue claims, but room function, household behavior, occupation
intensity, abandonment, ritual, daily life, and complete site-use maps require
separate custody.
