# Stonehenge and Acoustic Space Reconstruction Custody

The Stonehenge acoustic scale model is a RUINS case where the lost condition is
not a visible object but a sound environment. The current ruin lacks many
stones, so its present acoustics are not the same as the modelled prehistoric
layout. The reconstruction therefore depends on geometry, archaeological
evidence, scan data, scale modelling, chamber measurement, and inference.

The University of Salford reports that its Acoustics Research Centre built and
tested a 1:12 acoustic scale model of Stonehenge to determine how sound would
have been altered by all 157 original stones around 2200 BC. The Journal of
Archaeological Science article reports that scale modelling allowed Late
Neolithic and early Bronze Age acoustics to be quantified, with an average
mid-frequency reverberation time of 0.64 seconds and speech amplification of
about 4.3 dB. The paper says the model supports inference about musical sounds
and speech.

That evidence supports a strong acoustic-reconstruction claim: the model can
quantify likely acoustic effects under a reconstructed geometry. It does not
support direct ancient hearing. The model does not recover exact voices,
instruments, weather, crowd size, ground condition, ritual script, listener
positions, or builder intention. Speech amplification and reverberation are
measured effects, not a solved function.

The public memory risk is obvious. "Sounds of Stonehenge" framing makes the
model vivid, but RUINS keeps measurement, inference, demo, and ritual memory
apart. A modelled sound field can inform interpretation without becoming the
ancient event.
