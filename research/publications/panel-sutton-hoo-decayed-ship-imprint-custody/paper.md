# Sutton Hoo and the Decayed Ship Imprint

Sutton Hoo is not only a treasure case. It is a case where the central organic
structure vanished and left an evidentiary shadow: a ship imprint, rivet
pattern, chamber context, missing body problem, and durable grave goods. RUINS
should treat that imprint as a custody layer, not as the complete original ship.

The British Museum states that inside the mound was the imprint of a decayed
ship and a central chamber filled with treasures. It describes the burial as a
27 m ship with a chamber of high-status objects and also emphasizes uncertainty:
the identity of the person buried there cannot be named for certain, and acidic
soil left no bodily remains, though phosphate evidence indicated that a body had
once lain there.

The National Trust makes the vanished-fabric problem explicit. The 27 m
Anglo-Saxon ship no longer exists. It was made of oak and rotted away in acidic
soil, leaving only a "ghost" imprinted in sand. The same page separates the
Royal Burial Ground, eroded mounds, robbed mounds, the King's Mound treasure,
the British Museum display, a ship sculpture, and a reconstructed Mound Two.

This paper promotes decayed organic imprint custody. It separates the vanished
oak ship, soil imprint, rivets, chamber goods, missing body, phosphate
inference, mound erosion, robbing, display objects, replica/sculpture surfaces,
and royal/literary memory. Each layer supports a different claim grade.

The safe publication claim is narrow: Sutton Hoo is a strong RUINS case for
handling vanished organic fabric through imprint evidence. It is not a complete
ship reconstruction, a named-king proof, a full biography, a solved ritual
script, or a total account of Anglo-Saxon society.
