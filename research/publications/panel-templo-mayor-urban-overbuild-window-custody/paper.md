# Templo Mayor and the Excavation Window Problem

Templo Mayor is not just a ruin in a city. It is a buried city condition inside
a living capital, where public evidence appears through excavation windows,
museum objects, under-building fragments, construction rescues, and partial
exposure. RUINS should treat that mediation as the central evidence problem.

UNESCO frames Mexico City as a sixteenth-century Spanish city built on the ruins
of Tenochtitlan, with identified Aztec temple remains and later colonial and
modern public buildings. INAH describes the Templo Mayor Museum as the home for
objects recovered during the 1978-1982 excavations and notes that discoveries
continue through the Urban Archaeology Program. Those anchors support a modest
claim: the surviving evidence is strong for excavated remains, objects, and
identified fragments, but weak for any claim that a visible route equals the
complete sacred precinct or the whole buried city.

The Huei Tzompantli makes the problem sharper. INAH describes a fragment found
beneath a historic building during foundation work on Republica de Guatemala
24. The public can see an exposed northeast platform edge and a circular skull
structure, while INAH also states that the full size is unknown and that only
particular construction stages and samples have been identified. A window into a
buried structure is therefore not the structure as a whole.

This paper promotes urban overbuild window custody. It separates the layers that
often collapse in public knowledge: buried urban fabric, excavation window,
rescue context, removed object, museum display, digital scan, colonial reuse,
modern property, conservation monitoring, and later memory. Each can support a
different grade of claim. None can stand alone for the complete plan, ritual
program, conservation status, or city history.

The safe publication claim is narrow: Templo Mayor / Huei Tzompantli is a strong
case for how ruins survive through urban overbuild and mediated exposure. It is
not a complete reconstruction of Tenochtitlan, a solved inventory of the Sacred
Precinct, a full ritual explanation, or direct public access to original urban
fabric.
