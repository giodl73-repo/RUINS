# Tollund Man Preserved Body Custody

**Status:** publication-ready
**Track:** Evidence and Absence
**Capability:** `ruins-capability:preserved-body-taphonomy-custody`

## Abstract

Tollund Man looks immediately legible: a face, a body from a bog, a noose, a
last meal, and a museum encounter with the past. RUINS treats that vividness as
a risk. The preserved body is not a complete biography. It is a layered
survival chain: bog chemistry, discovery, excavation, forensic examination,
x-ray, autopsy, conservation choices, partial display, gut-content reanalysis,
and later ritual or execution interpretation.

## Evidence Layers

| Layer | Current claim | Limit |
|---|---|---|
| Find event | Discovered in Bjældskovdal bog in 1950 and first treated as possible recent murder. | Not a full excavation archive. |
| Preservation | Bog conditions preserved a face and body unevenly. | Preservation is not identity. |
| Forensics | Medical examiners concluded hanging by suffocation. | Cause is not motive. |
| Conservation | The head was preserved after excavation, autopsy, and dismemberment. | Display is not the original body state. |
| Gut contents | Reanalysis found porridge ingredients, possible fish, and parasite evidence. | Meal does not prove ritual meaning. |
| Comparative bog-body caution | Lindow Man shows preservation, conservation, and violent-death ambiguity. | Comparator, not Tollund identity. |
| Public memory | Face-to-face past, murder mystery, and sacrifice narratives shape reception. | Memory is afterlife. |

## Source-Locked Synthesis

Museum Silkeborg records the discovery as a body first reported to police, then
recognized as an archaeological find because it was deep in peat with no recent
digging. The museum also frames the encounter as face-to-face with the past.
Its forensic page supports hanging by suffocation while noting that the cervical
vertebrae were not broken. Its excavation page records the National Museum
excavation, x-ray, autopsy, dismemberment, and decision to preserve the head
rather than exhibit the whole body.

The Antiquity gut-content reanalysis adds a second layer: plant macrofossils,
pollen, non-pollen palynomorphs, steroid markers, and proteins support a meal
of porridge with barley, pale persicaria and flax, probably some fish, and
parasite evidence. The article allows fresh light on old questions; it does not
settle the reason for death. The British Museum Lindow Man record supplies a
parallel caution: bog chemistry preserves human tissue, conservation changes
displayable remains, and violent death can remain interpretively ambiguous.

## What RUINS Refuses

| Overclaim | RUINS response |
|---|---|
| A preserved face gives a complete biography. | It is a preserved surface within a damaged body and display history. |
| Hanging proves sacrifice or execution. | Cause of death and motive are separate layers. |
| Last meal proves ritual preparation. | Gut contents support diet evidence; ritual reading is gated. |
| The displayed head is the original body as found. | It is a conserved display outcome after excavation and autopsy choices. |
| Tollund Man represents all Iron Age bog bodies. | He is one case; comparisons require separate custody. |
| A modern museum encounter is direct access to the person. | It is public memory and display afterlife. |

## Method Result

Tollund Man activates preserved body taphonomy custody. The method is for cases
where survival of flesh, face, hair, stomach contents, or injury makes the dead
appear directly knowable. RUINS requires each layer to remain separate before
identity, cause, motive, ritual, or social history claims are made.
