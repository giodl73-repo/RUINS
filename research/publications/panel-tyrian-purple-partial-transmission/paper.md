# Tyrian Purple Partial Transmission

**Module:** RUINS
**Status:** working draft
**Date:** 2026-07-05

## Abstract

Tyrian purple is a RUINS case because an ancient technical tradition survives
through impressive but incomplete traces. Archaeology preserves crushed murex
shells, ceramic residues, workshop contexts, dyed textiles, analytical
chemistry, ancient descriptions, and royal-purple public memory. None of those
surfaces alone is a complete ancient recipe. RUINS treats purple dye as partial
technical transmission: enough survives to establish materials, colorant
identification, some procedural questions, and cultural significance, but not
enough to publish a settled operational method, universal production sequence,
or modern guide.

## Why This Tests The Capability

Roman concrete tested text/material/sample/experiment boundaries in a building
material. Tyrian purple tests the same capability in a biological dye
tradition, where evidence is distributed across:

- shell deposits,
- pigment residues on ceramics,
- preserved dyed textiles,
- HPLC and chemical markers,
- ancient textual descriptions,
- museum summaries,
- public royal/imperial memory,
- modern analytical and experimental reconstruction.

The danger is compression. "Murex purple" can become "the ancient recipe" too
quickly. RUINS instead asks which surface proves which claim.

## Evidence Layers

| Surface | Evidence condition | Current grade |
|---|---|---|
| Shell deposits | archaeological residue | possible production evidence only with context |
| Pigment residues on ceramics | material/chemical evidence | source-specific analytical claim |
| Dyed textiles | direct object evidence | source-specific use/identity claim |
| Ancient descriptions | textual/reporting evidence | process-description target, not recipe closure |
| Museum summaries | public educational synthesis | public-memory and overview claim |
| Modern experiments | reconstruction evidence | not ancient method unless explicitly bounded |

## Claim Grades

| Claim | Grade | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| Purple dye production can leave crushed shell, pigment-residue, and facility evidence. | Source-specific | PLOS Aegina supports this for one excavated context. |
| HPLC can identify murex-derived dye compounds on textile samples. | Source-specific | PLOS Timna supports this for tested samples. |
| Ancient and museum summaries describe laborious snail-based processing. | Public/textual summary | Not complete recipe custody. |
| Tyrian purple was universally made by one stable recipe. | Refuse | Technique likely varied by place, species, vessel, scale, and desired shade. |
| Royal purple memory proves every purple textile was royal. | Refuse | Status interpretation requires context. |
| Modern recreation proves ancient operational procedure. | Refuse | Reconstruction needs its own custody and status. |

## Partial-Transmission Ladder

1. Species and raw material may be identified.
2. Pigment or dye molecules may be chemically identified.
3. Workshop deposits may indicate production activity.
4. Ancient textual summaries may preserve process fragments.
5. Modern experiments may test plausibility.
6. Exact recipe, quantities, timing, vessels, labor, scale, and social use
   remain future-locked unless directly sourced.

## Refusals

RUINS refuses the following claims in this draft:

- a complete ancient Tyrian purple recipe;
- a universal production method for all murex-purple sites;
- exact production scale from shell heaps alone;
- social status from color alone;
- modern harvesting, extraction, dyeing, or manufacturing guidance;
- direct equivalence between modern experiments and ancient practice;
- claims that one site or textile defines the whole ancient dye economy.

## Conclusion

Tyrian purple is valuable for RUINS because it makes partial technique visible
without making it complete. The correct method is not to recover the lost
recipe. It is to keep each surviving surface in its own grade: shell, pigment,
textile, text, experiment, and memory.
