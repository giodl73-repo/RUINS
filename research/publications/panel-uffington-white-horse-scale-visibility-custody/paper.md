# Uffington and the Custody of a Re-Visible Hill Figure

Uffington White Horse tests the Nasca scale-visibility rule against a very
different surface survival. Nasca is a desert field made newly legible through
aerial, satellite, radar, and AI-assisted survey. Uffington is a chalk hill
figure whose visibility has survived through distance, slope, local scouring,
modern survey, and repeated restoration.

Historic England records the White Horse as a chalk-cut hill figure on a
west-facing slope, visible from several miles away, measuring about 111 metres
from tail to ear and about 40 metres high. It also records that geophysical
survey shows alteration in the head feature, that the horse was consolidated in
1936, and that it was temporarily covered during the Second World War. The
National Trust presents the site as an internationally renowned Bronze Age chalk
figure within a wider ancient landscape. English Heritage describes the project
evidence more specifically: drone survey and comparison with earlier drawings
and aerial photographs showed shrinkage in the white chalk lines, while earlier
OSL samples indicated a late Bronze Age or early Iron Age origin range.

These are strong claims, but they are not the same claim. A distant view proves
modern visibility from the vale. A drone survey documents current outline and
change. Restoration can make a figure easier to see, but it is not identical to
recovering the complete original shape. OSL sampling supports a date range, not
a final biography of the makers. Scouring and re-cutting preserve the monument
while also becoming part of its evidence history.

Uffington therefore promotes scale-mediated surface visibility custody to core
RUINS method. For large surface marks, the paper must keep ground trace,
viewpoint, current outline, restored outline, earlier drawings, aerial/drone
survey, maintenance, dating, documentary record, and interpretation in separate
custody. Otherwise the visible image becomes too persuasive: the reader starts
to treat the current white figure as the original form, the restoration profile
as complete recovery, or the emblem as a solved ancient meaning.

The responsible conclusion is narrow. Uffington shows that a surface mark can be
ancient, visible, maintained, altered, surveyed, restored, and culturally
remembered at once. RUINS owns the discipline that prevents those layers from
collapsing. It does not settle the horse's original symbolic meaning, the full
history of every cut, the exact final date, or the whole prehistory of the
Ridgeway landscape.
