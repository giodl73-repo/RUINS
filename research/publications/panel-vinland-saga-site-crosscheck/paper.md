# Vinland Saga Site Crosscheck

**Authors:** draft
**Status:** publication-ready
**Date:** 2026-07-05

## Abstract

Vinland is a RUINS case because two powerful evidence surfaces overlap without
becoming identical: Norse saga memory and the archaeological site at L'Anse aux
Meadows. The site gives direct material custody for Norse activity in North
America. The sagas preserve later-written literary traditions about westward
voyages. This paper argues that responsible reconstruction requires a
saga-memory crosscheck: site, text, route, name, date, and public discovery
claims must be graded separately.

## 1. Why This Is A RUINS Case

Vinland is not a simple case where archaeology proves a story. L'Anse aux
Meadows is a confirmed Norse archaeological site and a UNESCO World Heritage
property. Nature's 2021 dating work anchors Norse activity at the site to AD
1021. The sagas are later textual traditions that correspond with westward Norse
voyage memory but cannot automatically identify the site, route, actors, or
event sequence.

For current locked source boundaries, use [`source-lock.md`](source-lock.md) and
[`saga-site-crosscheck.md`](saga-site-crosscheck.md). The
[`route-name-quotation-gate.md`](route-name-quotation-gate.md) keeps the paper
publication-ready by refusing exact saga quotations, translations, route maps,
place-name identity claims, and named actor identifications.

## 7. Refusals

RUINS refuses the following claims in this paper:

- It will not treat L'Anse aux Meadows as all of Vinland.
- It will not treat the sagas as excavation records.
- It will not quote saga passages without direct edition and translation
  custody.
- It will not use AD 1021 to date all saga events.
- It will not turn Norse presence into Indigenous absence.
- It will not map routes or identify named actors without separate custody.

## 9. Conclusion

Vinland is useful to RUINS because it refuses a simple choice between saga and
site. L'Anse aux Meadows confirms Norse presence in North America; the sagas
preserve westward voyage memory. Responsible reconstruction keeps those claims
close enough to compare and separate enough not to falsify each other.
