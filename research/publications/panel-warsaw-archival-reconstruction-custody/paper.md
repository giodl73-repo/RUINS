# Warsaw and Archival Reconstruction Custody

Warsaw's historic centre is a RUINS case where destruction, records, surviving
fabric, expert interpretation, rebuilding, adaptation, and symbolic memory are
all part of the evidence condition. The rebuilt Old Town cannot be treated as
simple original survival, but neither is it merely a themed modern copy.

UNESCO describes the reconstruction as a coherent project devised by the Warsaw
Reconstruction Office between 1945 and 1951. The project used extant undamaged
structures, the late-medieval street and square network, reliable archival
documents, detailed iconographic and documentary records, pre-1939 and post-1944
conservation inventories, and expert knowledge from art historians, architects,
and conservators.

The same source keeps the reconstruction bounded. The target was a late
18th-century appearance, not every phase of the city's history. Some buildings
were deliberately not rebuilt. Street frontages and historic plots were retained,
but interiors and flats were revised to meet contemporary standards, while some
public interiors were recreated. The Warsaw Reconstruction Office archive is a
separate source-custody layer, not the reconstructed city itself.

This paper promotes archival reconstruction custody. It separates destruction
record, surviving fabric, archive, plan, painting, inventory, expert
interpretation, reconstruction project, deliberate adaptation, public symbolic
memory, and World Heritage recognition. The safe publication claim is narrow:
Warsaw is strong evidence for source-controlled urban reconstruction after
deliberate destruction, not complete original fabric, exact historical interior,
unbroken continuity, final conservation doctrine, or total national recovery.
