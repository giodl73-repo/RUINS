# Woodhenge and the Posthole Marker Problem

Woodhenge is a ruin where the main organic architecture is gone. What survives
for public knowledge is a patterned absence: crop marks, postholes, concrete
markers, a bank and ditch, deposited objects, and landscape relationships.
RUINS should treat the markers as evidence guides, not as timber posts.

English Heritage describes Woodhenge as a Neolithic timber monument built about
2500 BC, formed of six concentric ovals of standing posts, surrounded by a bank
and ditch. The site was discovered by aerial photography in 1925 when dark spots
and rings appeared in wheat. Excavation in 1926-7 showed that the dark spots
were postholes, and Ben and Maud Cunnington installed concrete posts to display
the positions of the vanished wooden posts.

The same source keeps function and form bounded. Woodhenge included six rings
of timber posts, the largest ring containing 16 posts about 35 centimetres in
diameter, but debate over whether it was an open-air structure, building, or
free-standing monument remains interpretive. Finds were placed in the ditch, by
posts, and into hollows left after posts rotted away; human remains were found,
but some child bones appear to have been lost after excavation.

UNESCO supplies the wider landscape rule: in the Stonehenge and Avebury World
Heritage property, stone and earth monuments retain original materials, while
timber structures have disappeared and postholes indicate their location.
Woodhenge is also named among monuments retaining solstice alignment, while the
original ceremonial use of the monuments is not known.

This paper promotes decayed organic imprint custody to Core. Woodhenge adds a
posthole-marker version of the Sutton Hoo problem: vanished organic structure
can be strongly visible without surviving as original fabric. The safe
publication claim is narrow: Woodhenge is a strong case for posthole-mediated
timber architecture, not a complete timber building, final ritual function, or
total Neolithic culture story.
