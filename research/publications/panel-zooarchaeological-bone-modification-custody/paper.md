# Bone Surface Modification and Zooarchaeological Custody

Zooarchaeological bone modifications can expose otherwise invisible behavior.
Cut marks, tooth marks, percussion scars, trampling striations, burning, and
breakage patterns can support bounded claims about carcass processing,
taphonomy, carnivore activity, marrow extraction, and assemblage formation.

The evidence is powerful but bounded. A cut mark is not an exact butchery event.
A tooth mark does not automatically identify a predator species. A mortality
profile is not by itself a kill-off strategy, and NISP/MNI counts are not a
complete diet. Interpretation depends on specimen context, taxon, skeletal
element, surface preservation, mark morphology, possible actors, timing,
recovery bias, fragmentation, age profile, experimental analogues, and whether
the inference concerns formation process or social behavior.

RUINS promotes zooarchaeological bone-modification custody to Core here. Bone
modification can support bounded taphonomic and behavioral pressure, but diet,
hunting, scavenging, feasting, ritual, site function, social organization, human
evolution, and complete subsistence systems require separate custody.
